# How to Pass Your Florida Daycare Inspection
*Plain-English guide. Written for real owners, not lawyers. Last updated 2026.*

## Who's knocking on your door
Your inspector works for the Florida Department of Children and Families (DCF), Office of Child Care Regulation. They are called a **Licensing Counselor**. Routine visits are unannounced. DCF requires **at least 2 unannounced inspections per year** for every licensed facility — the most aggressive routine schedule of any large state. Some counties — Hillsborough, Broward, Palm Beach, Pinellas, and Sarasota — run local licensing agencies that enforce DCF standards and may add extra rules.

- Agency: https://www.myflfamilies.com/services/licensing/child-care-licensure
- Public inspection search (CARES): https://caressearch.myflfamilies.com/PublicSearch

## The rulebook (this is what they measure you against)
- **The main law** — Florida Statutes Chapter 402, sections 402.301 through 402.319. [http://www.leg.state.fl.us/Statutes/index.cfm?App_mode=Display_Statute&URL=0400-0499/0402/0402.html](http://www.leg.state.fl.us/Statutes/index.cfm?App_mode=Display_Statute&URL=0400-0499/0402/0402.html)
- **The binding rules** — Florida Administrative Code Chapter 65C-22 (Child Care Standards). [https://www.flrules.org/gateway/ChapterHome.asp?Chapter=65c-22](https://www.flrules.org/gateway/ChapterHome.asp?Chapter=65c-22)
- **The Child Care Facility Handbook (October 2021)** — this is the one book every inspector uses in the field. It is incorporated by reference into 65C-22, which makes it legally binding.
- **The inspection form** — CF-FSP 5316 Standards Classification Summary. 32 categories the inspector scores on every visit.
- **The Child Care Programs and Inspections Guide (2023)** — DCF's own public explainer. [https://www.myflfamilies.com/sites/default/files/2023-07/Child-Care-Programs-and-Inspections-Guide.pdf](https://www.myflfamilies.com/sites/default/files/2023-07/Child-Care-Programs-and-Inspections-Guide.pdf)

## Your child-to-staff ratios (the #1 reason people fail)
| Age | Ratio |
|---|---|
| Birth – under 1 year | 1:4 |
| 1 – under 2 years | 1:6 |
| 2 – under 3 years | 1:11 |
| 3 – under 4 years | 1:15 |
| 4 – under 5 years | 1:20 |
| 5 years and older | 1:25 |

A ratio violation is a **Class 2** violation under F.S. § 402.305(4). Inspectors count heads on the spot. Do not round up.

## What the inspector actually does when they walk in
1. They arrive unannounced and sign in with the director.
2. They check that the license is posted.
3. They walk every classroom and count children against staff in each room.
4. They work through **CF-FSP 5316** — 32 inspection categories, one at a time.
5. They review child files using the Handbook checklist.
6. They review staff files and background screenings — every person listed in the Care Provider Background Screening Clearinghouse.
7. They review the daily **CF-FSP 5274** Health & Safety Checklist records.
8. They check the kitchen, playground, bathrooms, and diapering areas.
9. They close with an exit interview. Any finding that is not corrected gets a notice on **CF-FSP 5025**.

## The 5 things that fail most inspections in this state
1. **Ratio violations.** Class 2 under F.S. § 402.305(4). Handbook Section 2.
2. **Background screening gaps** in the Care Provider Background Screening Clearinghouse for a current employee.
3. **Missing or late DCF 40-hour introductory training** for staff past the 90-day window.
4. **Missing immunization record (DH Form 680 or 681) for an enrolled child.**
5. **Incomplete daily attendance / sign-in records** and incomplete daily CF-FSP 5274 checklists.

## Every child must have these papers in their file
- **CF-FSP 5131A** — Child Care Application for Enrollment.
- **Current immunization record** — Florida DH Form 680 or 681, or a valid exemption.
- **CF-FSP 5075** — Physician's Statement / physical.
- **Health history, allergy plan, and special-needs plan** where applicable.
- **Signed consents** for transportation, field trips, photography, and emergency medical care.
- **Daily sign-in / sign-out record.**
- **Incident reports** for any injury or illness.
- **Medication authorization and administration log.**
- **VPK paperwork** if this is a Voluntary Pre-K site.

## Every staff member must have these papers in their file
- **CF-FSP 5337** — Background Screening and Personnel File Requirements.
- **Level 2 background screening** cleared through the Care Provider Background Screening Clearinghouse (FDLE and FBI fingerprints, sex offender registry, local law enforcement, reference checks). **Required pre-hire.**
- **Florida DCF 40-Hour Introductory Child Care Training** — 30 hours core + 10 hours specialization — completed within the first 90 days.
- **10 hours annual in-service training** after the introductory 40.
- **Florida Child Care Staff Credential** for lead teachers.
- **Florida Director Credential** for directors.
- **CPR and First Aid** — at least one current staff on site at all times.
- **TB screening / health statement.**

## What must be on your walls
- **Current license** — posted where parents can see it.
- **Most recent inspection report** — required posting.
- **Fire evacuation plan** in each room.
- **Menu** — visible to parents.
- **Daily schedule** for each classroom.

## Drills and daily logs you can't skip
- **Daily CF-FSP 5274 Health & Safety Checklist** — filled out every operating day.
- **Daily sign-in / sign-out.**
- **Fire drills** monthly, documented.
- **Emergency / lockdown drills** per Handbook schedule, documented.
- **Incident reports** — same-day write-up, parent signature.
- **Medication log** for every dose administered.

## The 10-minute walk-through before the inspector arrives
1. Is the license posted and is the most recent inspection report posted?
2. Count each room. Ratio good right now?
3. Today's CF-FSP 5274 checklist — filled in?
4. This week's fire-drill log — documented?
5. Lock the cleaning chemicals. Lock the medications.
6. Is every infant sleeping on their back, in a crib, no blankets?
7. Pull three child files. Immunizations, physical, enrollment form — all there?
8. Pull three staff files. Background screening cleared in the Clearinghouse? 40-hour training done or within 90 days?
9. Is the daily sign-in current?
10. Is the kitchen clean, and is food storage labeled and dated?

## What happens if you fail
DCF classifies violations as **Class 1** (most serious, immediate risk), **Class 2** (serious — ratio violations are Class 2), or **Class 3** (less serious administrative). Enforcement escalates by class and by repeat-offense history. Sanctions include fines, probation, suspension, and revocation. Every inspection and every finding is posted to the public CARES record for that provider. Source: 65C-22 and F.S. Chapter 402.

## The weird Florida gotcha nobody tells you about
Your staff background checks live in the **Care Provider Background Screening Clearinghouse** — a statewide shared portal. When you hire someone who worked at another Florida daycare, their screening is already in the Clearinghouse and portable to you. But if your Clearinghouse roster does not match who is actually on your payroll, you will fail. On top of that, **5 counties** (Hillsborough, Broward, Palm Beach, Pinellas, Sarasota) have their own local licensing agencies that layer extra rules on top of DCF. Out-of-state operators miss this and get cited for a rule that does not exist statewide.

## For Magnus — how to sell to a Florida owner
**(3–5 sentences, private notes, not shown to customers in public versions.)** Open with: "DCF does 2 unannounced inspections a year — more than any major state. When's your next one?" That is the fear. Then mention the Child Care Facility Handbook by name and CF-FSP 5316 by form number — that proves you know the Handbook is the binding document, not just 65C-22. Biggest pain is the **Clearinghouse** not matching the actual payroll when an inspector checks — instant Class 2. Competitor today is usually brightwheel or Procare plus a binder; neither tracks the 40-hour intro training or the Clearinghouse roster. If they're in Hillsborough, Broward, Palm Beach, Pinellas, or Sarasota, lead with "we handle your local county's extra rules on top of DCF" — generic tools don't.
