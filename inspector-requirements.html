1The short version

If you remember nothing else, remember this grid.

  California (CDSS / CCLD) Florida (DCF) Texas (HHSC CCR)
Who inspects Licensing Program Analyst (LPA) DCF Licensing Counselor CCR Licensing Representative
Inspection cadence Annual unannounced visits; risk-based. Complaint visits any time. Minimum 2 unannounced visits/year (most aggressive). Unannounced visits at least annually; more for new permit holders.
Primary inspection instrument CARE Tools (Excel spreadsheets, one per facility type) + LIC 311A records list CF-FSP 5316 Standards Classification Summary (32 categories) Form 7259 (staff), 7260 (children), 7261 (center) evaluation forms
Violation system Type A (health/safety) vs Type B. Civil penalties via LIC 421. 4-class (1 = immediate danger, 4 = minimal). Class 1 can revoke license. Standards cited against Chapter 746; weighted by risk.

Record retention Licensing reports 3 years; sign-in sheets 30 days posted Fire drill records 12 months; attendance 12 months Attendance, drills, incidents — minimum 12 months on-site
The pattern Every state inspection boils down to three file stacks: (1) one file per child, (2) one file per staff member, (3) one file for the center itself. The inspector sits at a table, asks for each stack, opens a checklist form, and ticks boxes. The only thing that changes state-to-state is the form number, the field names, and the penalty ladder.

2California

Regulated by CDSS Community Care Licensing Division (CCLD), under Title 22 regulations.

CA · CDSS · CCLD
Who shows up
Licensing Program Analyst (LPA), from the regional CCLD office.
Legal backbone
Title 22, Division 12, Chapters 1 (Centers) and 3 (Family Child Care Homes).

LPA reviews file using LIC 9122A (pre-visit file review checklist) before arriving.
On-site tool
One of the CARE Tools — Excel workbooks, facility-type specific.

LIC 311A (centers) or LIC 311D (family homes) — the authoritative list of what must be on-site.

3Florida

Regulated by Florida DCF under Statute Ch. 402 and Administrative Code 65C-22. The Child Care Facility Handbook is incorporated by reference — it's legally binding.

FL · DCF · Licensing
Who shows up
DCF Licensing Counselor.
Cadence
Minimum 2 unannounced inspections per year plus pre-licensing (county health + fire marshal) and complaint-driven visits.
Primary form
CF-FSP 5316 — Standards Classification Summary. 32-page form, 32 categories, hundreds of line items, each pre-assigned a Class (1–4).
Home-based variant
CF-FSP 5274 — Registered Family Child Care Home Health & Safety Checklist.
Public visibility
Every finding enters CARES, parents can search.

Florida's 4-class violation system

ClassMeaningFine ladder
Class 1 Immediate danger — unsupervised child, firearm on premises, unscreened adult alone with children, unauthorized pickup, serious health/safety hazard $100–$500/day per violation. 3rd strike same standard → revoke license.
Class 2 Direct/potential threat — ratio violation, outlet covers missing, missing fire inspection, no monthly fire drill 2nd: $50 · 3rd: $60/day · 4th: probation + $75/day · 5th+: revoke + $100/day
Class 3 Low potential harm — license not posted, activity plan not posted, incomplete drill records 3rd: $25 · 4th: $30/day · 5th: probation + $40/day
Class 4 Indirect/minimal — still entered into CARES. Progressive corrective action.

Florida is the hardest state to pass clean 2× unannounced visits every year + every violation public in CARES + a 32-category form with hundreds of line items. A single Class 1 finding shows up on a parent's phone search. That's why FL is the best market to launch into — the pain is highest.

4Texas

Regulated by HHSC Child Care Regulation (CCR) under Chapter 746 Minimum Standards for Centers (Ch. 747 for Homes).

TX · HHSC · CCR
Who shows up
CCR Licensing Representative (a.k.a. inspector).
Cadence
Unannounced inspections at least annually; more frequent in the first year of a permit; complaint-driven any time.
Legal backbone
Chapter 746 Minimum Standards (Centers) / Chapter 747 (Homes).
Primary tools
Three interactive PDF evaluation forms: 7259 (staff files), 7260 (child files), 7261 (center records).

The three forms the inspector opens on arrival

Form 7259 Personnel Records Evaluation — Child Care Centers
Filled out by the inspector. Systematic per-staff checklist.
For every staff member, they verify:
  • Background check clearance (current)
  • Pre-employment affidavit (Form 2912) & employment affidavit (2985)
  • Training documentation (orientation, 24 clock hours/year of training, pre-service hours)
  • Health statement
  • Pediatric CPR / First Aid certification (current)
  • Professional development records
Form 7260 Children's Records Evaluation — Child Care Centers
Filled out by the inspector. Per-child checklist.
They pull individual files and verify:
  • Form 2935 Admission Information complete
  • Emergency contact info
  • Immunization records (Texas minimum state requirements)
  • Allergy & medical condition documentation
  • Parent authorizations (medication, authorized pickup, field trips)
  • Sign-in/sign-out logs present and complete
Form 7261 Center Records Evaluation
Filled out by the inspector. Center-level admin review.
They verify the presence of:
  • License / permit documentation
  • Form 2948 Plan of Operation
  • Form 2962 Verification of Liability Insurance
  • Fire / health / gas inspection records
  • Form 7263 Emergency Practices log
  • Posted menu & activity plans
  • Controlling persons documentation (Form 2760)
  • Any waivers / variances on file (Form 2937)

Home-based variants: Form 7270 (children) and Form 7271 (personnel) for LCCH/RCCH.

6What the product needs to do

This is the whole game. Every feature traces back to a line on a state form.

Core surfaces

  1. Three file-stack views: Children · Staff · Facility. That's how inspectors think, that's how the app should organize.
  2. "Inspection Ready" dashboard: a single screen showing green/yellow/red by state-specific form. For CA, it's the LIC 311A list. For FL, it's the 32 CF-FSP 5316 categories. For TX, it's Forms 7259/7260/7261 line items. The inspector should be able to flip through this and say "done."
  3. Expiry engine: CPR cards, TB tests, background checks, insurance, fire inspections, training hours. 30/60/90 day warnings pushed to director's phone.
  4. Daily walkthrough: TX Form 1100-style checklist that runs every morning on a tablet, auto-logs, produces a retained record. Same module serves FL Category 12 and CA's physical plant domain.
  5. Drill tracker: monthly fire, quarterly lockdown, quarterly severe weather, monthly detector tests. One log auto-generates TX Form 7263 / FL evacuation records / CA LIC 610 addenda.
  6. Real-time ratio monitor: sign-in/sign-out by child age bucket → current required staff count by room. Alert if a staff break would drop below threshold.
  7. Incident reporter: tablet form → auto-generates LIC 624 / CF-FSP incident / TX 7239. Parent signature capture. Attached to child record.
  8. Document capture & OCR: snap a photo of an immunization card, extract dates, auto-fill expiry. This single feature eliminates the binder.
  9. State form mapper: same logical document (e.g., "Parent consent for medical treatment") maps to LIC 627 / FL consent / TX authorization. User enters once; app prints the state-correct form.
  10. Parent portal: parents sign digitally, upload immunizations, update emergency contacts. Removes 80% of the admin's data-entry load.

7What actually happens during an inspection

The minute-by-minute playbook. Same structure in all three states — different forms on the clipboard.

Minute 0 — The inspector walks in unannounced

They do not call ahead. They do not email. They show up at the door, usually mid-morning when the facility is fully operating. They carry a laptop or iPad (CA: CARE Tool workbook open; FL: CF-FSP 5316 PDF; TX: Forms 7259/7260/7261 open).

First words — almost verbatim "Hi, I'm [name] with [DCF / CCLD / HHSC CCR]. I'm here to do a routine monitoring inspection. Can I speak with the director or person in charge?" They show a state-issued ID badge. The clock is now running.

Minute 1–5 — The entrance conference

  • Director escorts them to a quiet spot — usually the office.
  • Inspector states the scope: routine, complaint-driven, or re-inspection.
  • Asks: "Has anything changed since last visit? New staff? Capacity changes? Any incidents?"
  • Asks for the current staff roster and current child roster. This is the first document request.
  • CA: "Pull up your LIC 500 and LIC 9040." · FL: "Give me your current enrollment and staff list." · TX: "I need your 2947 list and your current roll."

Minute 5–30 — Physical walkthrough of the facility

The inspector walks every room with the director. They are looking, touching, smelling, and counting — not just reviewing paper. Expect them to:

  • Count heads and count staff in every room. Ratios are verified on the spot. If an aide just stepped to the restroom, that's a violation.
  • Open cabinets. Look for unlocked cleaning supplies, medications, sharp objects.
  • Check outlet covers, cord management, anchored furniture, choking hazards under age 3.
  • Confirm the license, evacuation plan, menu, activity schedule, and capacity signs are posted — in every room (FL).
  • Open the refrigerator. Check labeled bottles, dates on food, parent labels.
  • Check handwashing sinks, diapering stations, bleach solution concentration (they sometimes bring test strips).
  • Walk outdoor play area. Look at fencing height, fall zones under equipment, water hazards, locked gates.
  • Ask a random staff member: "What would you do if there was a fire right now?" They want to hear the plan, not read it.
  • Watch a diaper change, a meal, or a nap transition if timing lines up.
What they say when they see something wrong They don't scold. They take a photo. They write a code on their device. Then they say something like: "I'm noting this as a [Class 2 / Type A]. We'll go over it at the end." Don't argue on the spot — that's for the exit conference.

Minute 30–90 — The document deep-dive

This is where most time is spent, and this is where our product wins or loses the deal. The inspector sits at a table and says some version of:

Staff file request

  • "Pull files for these three staff." (random sample)
  • They open Form 7259 / CARE Staff Records / CF-FSP 5316 §25–28 on their screen.
  • For each staff:
    • "Show me the background check clearance."
    • "When was their last TB / physical?"
    • "Where's the CPR card — it expires when?"
    • "Show me proof of training hours this year."
    • "Signed mandated reporter statement?"
    • "Has this person's fingerprints cleared before they started?"

Child file request

  • "Pull files for these five children." (random sample, usually 10–20% of roster)
  • They open Form 7260 / LIC 311A §I / CF-FSP 5316 §29–30.
  • For each child:
    • "Show me the immunization record."
    • "Emergency contacts — is there more than one?"
    • "Who's authorized for pickup?"
    • "Medication authorization + administration log?"
    • "Allergies documented + posted in the classroom?"
    • "Signed consent for medical treatment?"
    • "Infant — where's the sleep plan, feeding plan?"

Facility file request

  • "Show me your most recent fire inspection certificate."
  • "Where are your drill logs for the last 12 months?" — they count drills, verify each month has fire, quarterly lockdown.
  • "Current liability insurance certificate?"
  • "Daily sign-in sheets — I want to see the last 30 days."
  • "Plan of operation. Who is the designated director in the director's absence?"
  • "Any incidents since last visit? Show me the incident reports."
  • CA: "Pull up your LIC 610 emergency plan and your LIC 9148."
  • FL: "Show me your written discipline policy." / "Your safe-sleep policy, signed by staff."
  • TX: "Show me Form 7263 with this month's extinguisher and CO tests initialed."
The phrases operators dread "I don't see a…" · "When was this last updated?" · "This is expired." · "Is there another copy?" · "Can you pull up [form] while I wait?" — Each of these is 30–90 seconds of fumbling in a filing cabinet, which an inspector interprets as disorganization. That frame biases every subsequent finding.

Minute 90–120 — Observation of practice

The inspector often lingers to watch actual practice that paper can't capture:

  • Sign-in/sign-out at pickup (is the parent signing? Is ID being checked for unfamiliar pickups?)
  • Meal service (are allergies visible? Is food being served at right temp?)
  • Nap transition (cribs spaced? sleep sacks only? infants on backs?)
  • Diapering (gloves, sanitizing, handwashing sequence)
  • Staff–child interactions (any yelling? appropriate redirection? any child left unattended even briefly?)

Minute 120 — The exit conference

This is the moment of truth. The inspector sits with the director and goes through findings one by one.

  • "I found [X] deficiencies today. Let me walk you through each."
  • Each violation is read aloud with its code: FL — "12.18, outlet covers not in place in the 3-year-old room, Class 2." TX — "Standard §746.3405, no documentation of monthly fire drill for February." CA — "Type A violation, staff file missing current TB clearance."
  • The director signs receipt (CA: LIC 9224; TX: LOC — Licensing Observation & Conclusion).
  • Inspector hands over (or emails) the written report. TX attaches Form 7277 Plan of Action for the director to fill out and return.
  • CA: inspector posts LIC 9213 Notice of Site Visit — it stays posted 30 days.
  • FL: findings go into CARES within 48 hours — publicly searchable.
The questions they always ask that people blow
  1. "Walk me through what happens if a child has an allergic reaction right now." — wants to see a plan, trained staff, medication on-site, signed authorization.
  2. "Who's in charge when the director is not here?" — must match LIC 308 / Plan of Operation / Form 2911.
  3. "When was your last lockdown drill? Show me." — date, time, staff initials, duration.
  4. "What's your ratio in this room right now?" — head count + age mix + required staff, on the spot.
  5. "How do you document medication administration?" — wants to see authorization + dose-by-dose log.

Follow-up — what comes after

  • Re-inspection window: 30–45 days depending on state and severity. Inspector returns specifically to check cited items.
  • Plan of Action (TX) or Corrective Action Plan (CA/FL) due within specified timeframe.
  • Public posting: Type A citations (CA), Class 1 violations (FL), any cited standards (TX) go public. Parents see them.
  • Civil penalty assessment: CA uses LIC 421; FL ladders per Class; TX depends on weighted severity.
Where the product makes the entire visit different When the inspector asks "show me X," the director opens our app on an iPad, filters by child/staff/form type, and turns the screen around. 30 seconds instead of 3 minutes. Every doc pre-verified green. Drill log auto-generated from monthly prompts. Ratio monitor showed 0 breaches in the last 90 days. The inspector leaves faster, with fewer findings, because the paper was never the problem — the organization of the paper was.

How to think about ordering

The pattern that travels Any industry where the shape is "facility gets inspected unannounced + file-per-employee + file-per-client/patient + expiry-driven credentials + paper-based violation findings" is in our lane. The product doesn't fundamentally change — only the form names, the checklist catalogs, and the state/federal violation codes differ. Build the core primitives state-configurable, and each new vertical is 90% content, 10% code.
  • Stay close to daycare first: preschool → after-school → adult day care → assisted living. Same regulatory agencies (CCLD/HHSC/DCF each regulate several of these).
  • Then pivot to healthcare credentialing: dental, home health, behavioral health. Higher ACVs, same primitives.
  • Long-tail opportunistic: restaurants, cannabis, trucking — lots of competition in each, but our "inspection-ready dashboard" framing is underrepresented vs. pure credential trackers.
