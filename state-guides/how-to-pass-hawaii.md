# How to Pass Your Hawaii Daycare Inspection
*Plain-English guide. Written for real owners, not lawyers. Last updated 2026.*

## Who's knocking on your door
In Hawaii, your licensor is a **DHS BESSD Child Care Licensing worker** from the island-specific unit covering your site. DHS (Department of Human Services, Benefit, Employment & Support Services Division) does licensing; **PATCH Hawaii** runs the DHS Early Childhood Registry that evaluates your staff's qualifications. You'll get a **pre-license inspection**, an **annual on-site monitoring visit**, and **unannounced follow-up visits** after any complaint. Fire, sanitation, and building inspections are done by county authorities (Honolulu, Hawaii, Maui, or Kauai).

- Hawaii DHS Child Care Licensing: https://humanservices.hawaii.gov/bessd/child-care-program/child-care-licensing/
- Public provider search: https://childcareprovidersearch.dhs.hawaii.gov/

## The rulebook (this is what they measure you against)
- **Statute:** HRS Chapter 346, Part V (§346-151 et seq.) — Child Care Facilities.
- **HAR 17-892.1** — Licensing of Group Child Care Centers and Group Child Care Homes (ages 2+).
- **HAR 17-895.1** — Licensing of Infant and Toddler Child Care Centers (6 weeks – 36 months).
- **HAR 17-891.2** — Registration of Family Child Care Homes.
- **HRS §302A-1154** — child immunization requirement.
- **HRS §346-153 / §346-153.5** — inspection records retention and public disclosure.

## Your child-to-staff ratios (the #1 reason people fail)
**Group Child Care Center (HAR 17-892.1) — ages 2+ ONLY:**

| Age | Staff:Child | Max Group |
|---|---|---|
| 2 years | 1:8 | 8 |
| 3 years | 1:12 | 12 |
| 4 years | 1:16 | 16 |
| 5+ years | 1:20 | 20 |

**Infant and Toddler Center (HAR 17-895.1) — separate license:**

| Age | Ratio | Max Group |
|---|---|---|
| 6 weeks – 12 months | 1:3 to 1:4 | 6 – 8 |
| 12 – 24 months | 1:3 to 1:4 | 6 – 12 |
| 18 – 36 months | 1:5 to 1:6 | 10 – 12 |

**Family Child Care Home:** 1:2 for under-12-month infants; 1:6 for older kids; 6 total.

**Warning:** The **youngest child in the room sets the ratio** for mixed-age groups. If you hold a Group Center license, you **cannot** legally serve kids under 24 months — they require a separate Infant/Toddler Center license.

## What the inspector actually does when they walk in
1. Arrives (pre-license by appointment; annual and complaint visits often unannounced).
2. Confirms current DHS license is posted.
3. Walks every room, counts children and staff, checks that every ratio-counted staff has a current PATCH registry listing.
4. Pulls staff files — PATCH registry, Form 314-C criminal/CANIS clearance, TB clearance, CPR/First Aid, 16-hour annual in-service log.
5. Pulls child files — enrollment app, immunization record, TB clearance, Form 14 health assessment, emergency consent.
6. Reviews fire drill log, emergency plan, medication log, attendance.
7. Writes up any deficiencies with a corrective action plan.

## The 5 things that fail most inspections in this state
1. **Staff counted in ratio without a current PATCH Early Childhood Registry listing** — per HAR 17-892.1, staff must have an active PATCH evaluation before counting.
2. **Infant and toddler care offered under the wrong license** — mixing under-24-month kids into a Group Center is an automatic violation (HAR 17-895.1 requires a separate license).
3. **Missing or expired TB clearance** — unusual for the U.S. but required for staff AND children in Hawaii (HRS Chapter 321-22).
4. **Incomplete immunization records** — per HRS §302A-1154 and DOH requirements (DTaP, IPV, MMR, Hep B, Varicella, Hib, PCV).
5. **Background-check gaps** — especially Form 314-C clearance for volunteers and household members 18+ (HHS-OIG flagged this across Hawaii in its 2020 audit).

## Every child must have these papers in their file
- Enrollment application with emergency contacts and authorized pickup.
- **Immunization record** per HRS §302A-1154 / DOH requirements.
- **TB clearance** — physician-signed **Form 314** before entry.
- **Form 14** health assessment / physical on admission.
- Emergency medical authorization.
- Food allergy and dietary restriction notes.
- Signed field-trip and transportation permissions.
- Daily attendance log.
- Accident / incident reports.

## Every staff member must have these papers in their file
- **DHS Early Childhood Registry** listing (PATCH-issued, verified).
- **Criminal history check** (state & FBI fingerprint) + **CANIS** (Child Abuse & Neglect Registry) clearance — required for staff, volunteers, and household members 18+.
- **TB clearance** (annual/biennial per DOH).
- **Pediatric CPR & First Aid** — current.
- **Annual 16-hour in-service training log**.
- Orientation and mandated-reporter records.
- Degree / ECE credential copy (lead teachers).

## What must be on your walls
- Current DHS license.
- County fire inspection approval.
- County sanitation / health approval.
- Evacuation and emergency procedures.
- Daily schedule.
- Menu (if meals served).
- DHS complaint contact info for parents.

## Drills and daily logs you can't skip
- **Fire drills** — documented per HAR requirements.
- **Tsunami / earthquake / severe weather drills** — documented (Hawaii-specific hazards).
- **Daily attendance** — every room.
- **Medication administration log**.
- **Incident / injury reports**.
- **Feeding and sleep logs** for infants (Infant/Toddler Centers).

## The 10-minute walk-through before the inspector arrives
1. Count kids and staff per room; confirm every ratio-counted staff has current PATCH status.
2. Check that no under-24-month kids are in a Group-Center-only room.
3. Post the current license and the two most recent county inspections.
4. Confirm all staff TB clearances and Form 314-C background checks are current.
5. Pull a random child file — does it have Form 14, immunizations, and Form 314 TB?
6. Medication cabinet locked. Log up to date.
7. Diaper-change stations clean; gloves stocked; handwashing sink works.
8. Playground gates latch. No standing water.
9. Fire drill log — last one within required interval.
10. Sand/sandpit covered overnight (common Hawaii citation).

## What happens if you fail
DHS can issue a deficiency citation with a required corrective-action plan. Repeated or serious findings can lead to a **conditional license**, **suspension**, or **revocation** under HRS Chapter 346. HRS §346-153 requires DHS to keep inspection results, deficiency citations, and corrective action plans for **two prior years plus current** and make them "available for the public to review" — though Hawaii's portal does not yet display them online, anyone can request them from your island's licensing unit. You can appeal enforcement actions through DHS administrative hearings and ultimately to state court.

## The weird Hawaii gotcha nobody tells you about
**Hawaii is the only state where PATCH — not DHS — decides whether your staff member is "qualified."** A new hire can clear her background check, have her First Aid, and still not count toward ratio until PATCH evaluates her education and issues a Registry level. That evaluation takes weeks. Owners who hire "Monday start" without having filed the PATCH application two months earlier walk into their next inspection out of ratio on paper. Second gotcha: HRS §346-153 **requires** DHS to maintain 2 years of inspection records for public review, but the consumer portal only shows license status — so a Civil Beat or UIPA records request can still surface your full history to a curious parent.

## For Magnus — how to sell to a Hawaii owner
Their fear is a parent filing a complaint with DHS that then becomes public via UIPA. The 30-second number: every ratio-counted staff member needs an active PATCH Early Childhood Registry listing — owners routinely hire and put someone on the floor before PATCH clears them, which is an instant ratio violation. Hawaii owners today track PATCH expirations, TB clearances, and Form 314-C background checks on spreadsheets or sticky notes. Lead with: "One dashboard that shows every staff member's PATCH level, TB clearance, and background check so nobody on your floor is out of compliance when the licensing worker shows up." Bonus angle: Hawaii was cited by HHS-OIG in 2020 for background-check gaps — the state is under federal pressure.
