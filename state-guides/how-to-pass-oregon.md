# How to Pass Your Oregon Daycare Inspection
*Plain-English guide. Written for real owners, not lawyers. Last updated 2026.*

## Who's knocking on your door
Your inspector is a **licensor** from the **Oregon Department of Early Learning and Care (DELC), Child Care Licensing Division (CCLD)**. DELC replaced the old Early Learning Division in 2023. One licensor is assigned to every certified center in the state. They must visit **at least once a year, and that visit is usually unannounced**. They also come out any time a parent files a complaint. Every visit they file — and every finding they make — is posted on the public **Find Child Care Oregon** portal, where any parent can see your history for the last 5 years.

- Agency: https://www.oregon.gov/delc/
- Public inspection portal: https://findchildcareoregon.org/

## The rulebook (this is what they measure you against)
- **This is the law:** ORS 329A.250 – 329A.500 (Oregon Child Care Act). https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_chapter_329a
- **This is the general rulebook for every facility:** OAR Chapter 414, Division 205 (CCLD-0731)
- **This is the center rulebook (ratios live here):** OAR Chapter 414, Divisions 300 & 305 (CCLD-0084). https://www.oregon.gov/delc/providers/CCLD_Library/CCLD-0084-Rules-for-Certified-Child-Care-Centers-EN.pdf
- **This is the background-check rule:** OAR Chapter 414, Division 061 (Central Background Registry / CBR)
- **This is the inspection form:** CCLD-0093 Child Care Facility Contact Report. https://www.oregon.gov/delc/providers/CCLD_Library/CCLD-0093-Contact-Report-SAMPLE.pdf
- **This is the health & safety checklist:** CCLD-0090

## Your child-to-staff ratios (the #1 reason people fail)

For centers first licensed **after July 15, 2001** (Table 3A in OAR 414-305-0400):

| Age | One staff for this many kids | Max group size |
|---|---|---|
| 6 weeks to 24 months | 1:4 | 8 |
| 24 months to 36 months | 1:5 | 10 |
| 36 months to school-age | 1:10 | 20 |
| School-age | 1:15 | 30 |

Centers licensed **on or before July 15, 2001** (grandfathered, Table 3B): infants/toddlers up to 30 months 1:4 (group 8), 30 months to school-age 1:10 (group 20), school-age 1:15 (group 30). You must pick one table — no mixing.

Source: OAR 414-305-0400. https://oregon.public.law/rules/oar_414-305-0400

**Warning:** Inspectors count heads on the spot. Ratios apply during nap, meals, outdoor play, and transitions. Do not round up. At least one caregiver in every group must meet "Teacher" qualifications under OAR 414-305-0340.

## What the inspector actually does when they walk in
1. Arrives unannounced and shows state ID.
2. Checks your posted certificate, Spark rating (if any), and emergency plan.
3. Walks each room and counts kids vs. staff against the ratio table you follow (3A or 3B).
4. Pulls a sample of staff files: **Central Background Registry (CBR) enrollment**, Safe Sleep training (infant-room staff), CPR/First Aid, food handler card (kitchen staff), annual training log.
5. Pulls a sample of child files: enrollment (PR-0185), immunization record, health history, emergency contacts.
6. Reviews fire drill log, injury/incident log, and medication log.
7. Fills out **CCLD-0093 Contact Report** on the spot.
8. Exit interview: reads out any **valid findings** and corrective actions, with due dates.

## The 5 things that fail most inspections in Oregon
1. **Ratio not met** (OAR 414-305-0400). Top citation statewide.
2. **Staff working before Central Background Registry enrollment is approved** (OAR 414-061). No CBR = no unsupervised child contact. Ever.
3. **Safe Sleep for Oregon Infants training missing** for infant-room staff (must be completed within 30 days of hire).
4. **Annual training hours short** — rule requires 15 clock hours/year, with ≥8 in child development or ECE and ≥1 in health/safety/nutrition (OAR 414-305-0380).
5. **Food handler card missing** for kitchen/cook or infant-room bottle prep staff (within 30 days of hire).

## Every child must have these papers in their file
- **PR-0185 Child Enrollment Form** (child name, DOB, parents, emergency contacts, authorized pickups)
- **Immunization record** that meets ORS 433.267 (or valid exemption)
- Health history and allergy info
- Medication authorization and administration log
- Signed authorizations for transportation, field trips, photos
- Daily attendance (sign-in / sign-out)
- Incident and injury reports
- Individualized care plan for any special needs child

Records must be kept **at least 2 years** after enrollment ends.

## Every staff member must have these papers in their file
- Application, résumé, qualification verification (Teacher, Aide I/II, Director per OAR 414-305-0320 to 0360)
- **Central Background Registry (CEN-0001)** enrollment approved *before* first day of unsupervised contact
- Pediatric CPR and First Aid (current)
- Safe Sleep for Oregon Infants — for infant-room staff, within 30 days
- Recognizing and Reporting Child Abuse training
- Food Handler Card — for food-handling staff, within 30 days
- Introduction to Child Care Health & Safety + Child Development (within 90 days)
- Annual training log — **15 hours/year**
- Oregon Registry (ORO) record

## What must be on your walls
- Current certification and any Spark rating
- Posted ratios and group-size chart
- Emergency evacuation map (each room)
- Fire drill log
- Weekly menu
- Parent complaint notice with CCLD number (1-800-556-6616)

## Drills and daily logs you can't skip
- **Fire drills** — monthly, documented
- **Earthquake / shelter-in-place** — as specified in your emergency plan
- **Daily attendance** log
- **Medication** log
- **Injury/incident** log
- **Lead-in-water testing** on file (Oregon requires drinking water testing)

## The 10-minute walk-through before the inspector arrives
1. Count kids and staff in every room. Fix ratio first.
2. Confirm license, ratios, emergency map are posted.
3. Pull this month's fire drill entry — is it logged?
4. Open three staff files: CBR approval letter, CPR card, Safe Sleep (infant rooms), training log.
5. Open three child files: enrollment form, immunizations, emergency contacts.
6. Playground: no broken equipment, no standing water, fence intact.
7. Cribs: flat back, firm mattress, nothing in the crib, one infant per crib.
8. Cleaning chemicals, medications, sharps locked up.
9. Water bottles accessible, diaper table disinfected.
10. Greet the inspector. Offer a chair. Be pleasant and factual.

## What happens if you fail
A licensor records a **Valid Finding** when a reasonable person would conclude non-compliance happened. You get a noncompliance letter, and you must post it at the facility for **12 months** and it stays on the Safety Portal for **5 years**. Escalation goes from correction plan → civil money penalty → conditional license → suspension → revocation. Serious injuries, deaths, and substantiated abuse counts also publish. You can request review of a finding in writing within the deadline on the notice.

## The weird Oregon gotcha nobody tells you about
Oregon publishes **two different statuses for every complaint**: "Valid Finding" (the non-compliance is proven) and "Unable to Substantiate" (conflicting or insufficient evidence). Both appear on your public record — the **"unable to substantiate" ones for 2 years, the valid findings for 5 years**. That means a parent who files a false complaint can still create a public entry on your profile, even when you're cleared. Lesson: document everything. If a parent threatens a complaint, write the incident down that day. Keep the sign-in sheet, the camera footage, the text messages. The best defense when a complaint lands is a binder of contemporary notes.

## For Magnus — how to sell to an Oregon owner
Their fear is a **public "Valid Finding"** on Find Child Care Oregon that stays for 5 years. Drop the **Central Background Registry** deadline (no unsupervised contact until CBR is approved) in 30 seconds — most OR owners have a drawer of paper CBR letters and no system tracking the 2-year renewal. Say "I can show you every staff member's CBR status, Safe Sleep training, and the 15 annual training hours in one screen." Portland / Eugene / Salem owners are the highest-density target; Oregon also has unusually rich public violation data so lead magnets ("we pulled every Valid Finding in your ZIP — here are the 3 most common") land hard.
