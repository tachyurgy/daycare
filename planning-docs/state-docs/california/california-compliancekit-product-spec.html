Section 02

The Inspector & Inspection Process

How Licensing Program Analysts (LPAs) evaluate facilities using the CARE Tools framework.

Who Inspects

Licensing Program Analysts (LPAs) — state employees under CCLD. Each LPA is assigned a caseload of facilities within their regional office territory. They are trained on the CARE Tools framework and carry regulatory authority to issue citations, impose fines, and recommend license actions.

Inspection Types

Four distinct triggers bring an LPA to a facility:

  • Annual/Routine — scheduled compliance review
  • Random Unannounced — spot checks without prior notice
  • Complaint-Driven — always unannounced, triggered by filed complaints
  • Follow-Up — verifying corrective actions from prior citations

What Happens During an Inspection

1

LPA Arrives at Facility

May or may not give advance notice for routine inspections. Complaint-driven visits are always unannounced.

2

Posts Notice of Site Visit

LPA posts LIC 9213 (Notice of Site Visit) at the facility entrance, visible to parents.

3

Full Facility Tour

All areas accessible to children are walked and inspected, including outdoor spaces.

4

Physical Safety Inspection

Checks cabinets, closets, storage areas, locks/latches, exterior perimeter, all equipment and surfaces.

5

Records Review

All required records are reviewed for accuracy, completeness, and currency. This is where most citations originate.

6

Verify Posted Items

Confirms all 10 required items are posted, up-to-date, and visible to parents and staff.

7

Ratio & Capacity Verification

Verifies staff-child ratios and licensed capacity on the spot by counting children and staff present.

8

CARE Tools Evaluation

Uses the CARE Tools framework to systematically evaluate compliance across all applicable domains.

9

Document Findings & Issue Citations

All findings are documented. Citations (Type A or Type B) are issued if warranted, with corrective action timelines.

Section 03

Citation Classification System

California uses a two-tier Type A / Type B citation system with progressive enforcement escalation.

Type A Critical — Immediate Threat to Health, Safety, or Personal Rights

  • Fine: $50/violation/day, escalating up to $150/violation/day until corrected
  • Death or serious injury: Escalated penalties apply
  • Posting requirement: Must be posted at facility for 30 days, visible to parents
  • Compliance/non-compliance documentation: Also posted for 30 days
  • Examples: Unsupervised children, hazardous conditions, background check violations, corporal punishment

Type B Warning — Potential Threat if Uncorrected

  • First occurrence: Corrective action required within specified timeframe; no immediate fine
  • 2nd citation within 12 months: $150/violation + $150/day until corrected
  • 3rd citation within 12 months: $150/violation + $150/day + potential probation or license action
  • Examples: Missing records, expired certifications, minor posting deficiencies

Progressive Enforcement Ladder

When citations accumulate or remain uncorrected, CCLD escalates through these enforcement actions:

Probation — Facility continues operating under heightened monitoring and conditions
License Suspension — Temporary halt of operations; may be reinstated after compliance
License Revocation — Permanent removal of license; facility must cease all operations
Temporary Suspension Order — Immediate shutdown when children face imminent danger
Exclusion of Individuals — Specific persons banned from facility contact with children
Section 04

Top 10 Most Common California Citations

Analysis of enforcement data across 28,000+ facilities. These are the violations ComplianceKit must prevent.

1

Background Check Gaps

17.1% of all facilities cited. Missing or incomplete LiveScan fingerprinting, DOJ/FBI/CACI clearance gaps, expired Criminal Record Statements.

2

Staff Training Deficiencies

10.3% of centers cited. Incomplete 16-hour health & safety training, expired CPR/first aid cards, missing water safety certificates.

3

Building Safety Standards

9.1% of FCCHs cited. Structural deficiencies, fire safety violations, accessibility issues, hazardous materials storage.

4

Required Postings Missing

7.8% of centers cited. License, menus, activity schedules, Personal Rights poster, or Parent's Rights poster not posted or outdated.

5

Mandated Reporter Training

4.8% of facilities. Surging 187% in 2026 due to new enforcement emphasis. 2-year renewal cycle frequently missed.

6

Supervision Violations

Children left unsupervised or inadequately supervised. Among the most likely to be classified as Type A (critical).

7

Incomplete Children's Records

One or more of the 21 required child record items missing, expired, or unsigned. Common gaps: immunization records, emergency contacts, health evaluations.

8

Sanitation / Diapering Violations

Improper diapering procedures, handwashing gaps, sanitation of surfaces and equipment.

9

Medication Administration Gaps

Missing parental consent forms (LIC 9221), improper storage, incomplete dispensing logs (LIC 622).

10

Playground Safety / Fall Surfacing

Inadequate fall zones, non-compliant surfacing materials, equipment in disrepair, age-inappropriate equipment access.

Product Insight: 7 of the top 10 citations are documentation and training tracking issues — exactly the category of problems software solves best. ComplianceKit's core value proposition maps directly to the most frequent violation types.

Section 11

Why California Is the Biggest Opportunity

Strategic rationale for launching in California first. Every metric points to the highest-leverage market in the country.

40,000+

Largest Addressable Market

More licensed child care facilities than any other state. Single-state go-to-market captures the biggest possible wedge of the national market.

35+

Overwhelming Paper Burden

35+ unique LIC forms per facility. The sheer volume of paperwork creates unbearable manual burden and strong product-market fit for digital compliance.

21 + 19

Complexity That Demands Software

21 child record items and 19 staff record items per person. No spreadsheet or file cabinet can reliably track this many compliance touchpoints with expiry dates.

30 days

Reputation Risk Drives Urgency

Type A citations must be publicly posted for 30 days. Parents see them. This reputation risk creates powerful motivation to invest in prevention.

21

Regional Office Inconsistency

21 regional offices means 21 variations in enforcement emphasis. Providers need a single source of truth that tracks the superset of all requirements.

CA-only

Earthquake Prep = Competitive Moat

LIC 9148 Earthquake Preparedness is California-specific. Building this into ComplianceKit demonstrates deep vertical knowledge that generic tools can never match.

Cross-State Architecture Notes

How California fits into the multi-state ComplianceKit platform. Design principles for state-specific extensibility.

≡

State-Specific Regulation Engine

Each state loads its own rule set: forms, ratios, timelines, violation classifications, and enforcement procedures. California's module includes all 38 LIC/PUB forms and Title 22 requirements.

⇄

Unified Data Model

Core entities (Child, Staff, Facility, Inspection, Citation) are shared across all states. State-specific extensions add fields and validation rules without forking the schema.

▣

Multi-State Dashboard

Operators with facilities in TX, FL, and CA can manage compliance across all three states from one dashboard, with state context switching and unified alerts.

✓

Onboarding State Selector

During onboarding, selecting "California" pre-loads all 38 form templates, California ratios, Title 22 regulation references, and CCLD reporting requirements.
