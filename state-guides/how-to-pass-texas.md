# How to Pass Your Texas Daycare Inspection
*Plain-English guide. Written for real owners, not lawyers. Last updated 2026.*

## Who's knocking on your door
Your inspector works for the Texas Health and Human Services Commission (HHSC), Child Care Regulation (CCR) Division. They are state employees. They carry a laptop or tablet running the **CLASS system** and type deficiencies in real time during the visit. Routine annual visits are a full day and are unannounced. Every deficiency they write is posted on the public operation page within 14 days.

- Agency: https://www.hhs.texas.gov/services/family-safety-resources/child-care
- Public inspection search: https://childcare.hhs.texas.gov/

## The rulebook (this is what they measure you against)
- **The main law** — Texas Human Resources Code, Chapter 42. This is the statute that gives HHSC its authority. [https://statutes.capitol.texas.gov/Docs/HR/htm/HR.42.htm](https://statutes.capitol.texas.gov/Docs/HR/htm/HR.42.htm)
- **The Minimum Standards for Child-Care Centers** — 26 TAC Chapter 746. This is the rulebook for centers. [https://texreg.sos.state.tx.us/public/readtac$ext.ViewTAC?tac_view=4&ti=26&pt=1&ch=746](https://texreg.sos.state.tx.us/public/readtac$ext.ViewTAC?tac_view=4&ti=26&pt=1&ch=746)
- **Minimum Standards for Child-Care Homes** — 26 TAC Chapter 747 (Licensed and Registered homes).
- **Minimum Standards for School-Age and Before/After-School Programs** — 26 TAC Chapter 744.
- **Licensing procedure rules** — 26 TAC Chapter 745 (applications, deficiencies, enforcement, background checks).
- **Published Minimum Standards page** — https://www.hhs.texas.gov/providers/child-care-regulation/minimum-standards

Inspectors count heads on the spot. Do not round up. Ratios must be met at all times — including transitions, outdoor play, and field trips.

## What the inspector actually does when they walk in
1. They show up unannounced and ask for the director.
2. They check the license is posted.
3. They walk every classroom with a laptop or tablet open to CLASS.
4. They count children against adults in each room.
5. They pull staff files and work through **Form 7259** — Personnel Records Evaluation.
6. They pull children's files and work through **Form 7260** — Children's Records Evaluation.
7. They pull center records on **Form 7261** — Center Records Evaluation.
8. They review the **Plan of Operation (Form 2948)** and the daily **Form 1100** building and grounds checklists.
9. They close with an exit meeting and a deficiency list.

## The 5 things that fail most inspections in this state
1. **Ratio violations.** Chapter 746 — High deficiency weight every time.
2. **Background-check gaps.** A staff member without cleared fingerprints, DPS criminal history, or Central Registry — Chapter 745 Subchapter F.
3. **Pre-service training not done.** 24 hours pre-service is required, and 8 hours must be done **before** the person works with children.
4. **Missing daily Form 1100** — the 24-item building and grounds checklist. Inspectors look for the completed sheets for this week.
5. **Plan of Operation out of date** — Form 2948 must be updated at least annually and match what you actually do.

## Every child must have these papers in their file
- **Form 2935** — Admission Information (parents, emergency contacts, authorized pickups).
- **Immunization record** matching the Texas Department of State Health Services schedule, or a valid exemption.
- **Health statement / physical exam.**
- **Hearing and vision screening records** (for age-required children).
- **Individual health or allergy plan** if applicable.
- **Signed consents** for transportation, field trips, photography, and emergency treatment.
- **Form 2941** — Sign-In / Sign-Out log.
- **Form 7239** — Incident / Illness Reports, as needed.
- **Medication authorization and administration log.**

## Every staff member must have these papers in their file
- **Personal history statement.**
- **Background check** — Texas DPS criminal history, FBI fingerprint, and Central Registry check. Must be cleared **before** the person starts work.
- **Health statement and TB screening.**
- **24 hours pre-service training** — with 8 hours completed before working with children.
- **24 hours per year in-service training.**
- **CPR, First Aid, and pediatric AED** certification.
- **SIDS prevention training** if serving infants.
- **Mandated reporter training.**
- **Qualification documents** (education and experience) per 26 TAC 746 Subchapter D.

## What must be on your walls
- **Current license** — posted where parents can see it.
- **Most recent inspection** — publicly available; many operators post a printed copy too.
- **Menu** — visible to parents.
- **Emergency exit plan** in every classroom.
- **Daily schedule and age-group assignments.**

## Drills and daily logs you can't skip
- **Form 1100** — 24-item daily building and grounds checklist, Monday through Friday. Keep a stack filled out.
- **Form 2941** — sign-in/sign-out for every child, every day.
- **Form 7239** — incident/illness reports when needed, same day.
- **Fire drill** and **emergency drills** per Chapter 746 — documented.
- **Form 7263** — Emergency Practices documentation.

## The 10-minute walk-through before the inspector arrives
1. Check that the license is posted.
2. Count each room. Do you meet ratio right now?
3. Pull today's Form 1100. Is every box checked and initialed?
4. Pull this week's Form 2941 sign-in sheets.
5. Lock the cleaning-chemical and medication cabinets.
6. Check every outlet cover in infant and toddler rooms.
7. Pull three staff files at random. Are background checks cleared? Are training hours tracked?
8. Pull three child files at random. Is every admission and immunization document there?
9. Is your Plan of Operation (Form 2948) current?
10. Are the last fire drill and emergency drill records filed?

## What happens if you fail
Texas uses a **deficiency weight system**: **High, Medium-High, Medium, Medium-Low, Low**. The weight drives enforcement and the size of any administrative penalty. A finding flows through a progression: deficiency → evaluation → probation → suspension → revocation. You have 30 days after a penalty notice to request a hearing. Every deficiency is posted on your public operation page at childcare.hhs.texas.gov within 14 days. Source: 26 TAC Chapter 745 Subchapter M.

## The weird Texas gotcha nobody tells you about
Every deficiency you get is **publicly searchable within 14 days** at childcare.hhs.texas.gov — with full narrative, Minimum Standard citation, and weight. This is one of the most transparent state datasets in the country. Parents Google your operation before dropping their kid off. One Medium-High finding for a ratio violation is readable forever. On top of that, Texas has a fourth tier of operator that most people never hear about: the **Listed Family Home**, for caregivers with just 1–3 unrelated children — you still have to be listed with HHSC.
