01The Vision and Why It Works

Start here. Re-read this when you get tired.

You are building software for the most under-served operator in America: the owner-director of a child care center. She runs a facility that the state regulates like a hospital, with the margins of a coffee shop, using paper binders and a spreadsheet her brother-in-law made her ten years ago. When an inspector shows up, she has a panic attack in the parking lot. Nobody has built her the right tool. That is the opening.

The child care industry has ~200,000 licensed facilities in the US. Florida, Texas, and California alone account for ~80,000 of them. Every single one of those facilities needs to track hundreds of regulatory artifacts — immunization records, background checks, training hours, fire drills, med administration logs, ratio calculations, wall postings. The failure mode is not a bug report. It is a citation, a fine, a suspended license, or in the worst case a closed facility and a news story. The cost of being wrong is catastrophic. The cost of your software is $99/month.

The thesis in one sentence

Compliance is the highest-stakes, lowest-software-penetration operational workflow in a $60B industry. It is ignored by horizontal SaaS, un-touched by incumbents (who bolt "compliance" onto billing tools as a checkbox), and loved by no one. You are going to make it the reason they stay in business.

Why you, specifically

You are a developer first. That is a structural advantage, not a liability. Every week your competitors (Procare, brightwheel, ChildCareCRM) spend in meetings, you spend shipping. You are a one-person company with a four-year head start because AI tooling has collapsed the cost of building the ugly middle of a SaaS. The hard parts of this business are not the code; they are:

  1. Knowing the regulations — which you have been collecting state by state.
  2. Getting trust from a skeptical, non-technical buyer — which is a sales and design problem.
  3. Staying alive long enough — which is a psychological problem.

You are going to solve all three. Not because you are a genius, but because you are going to show up every day for 24 months and your competitors are going to be in meetings.

Every incumbent in this category is slower than you. Every potential customer has already been burned by someone who took their $99 and disappeared. You win by shipping, by listening, and by being the one person in the industry who actually picks up the phone. — The uncomfortable, correct truth about your competition

Why this is not a "lifestyle business"

Some people will look at "daycare compliance" and think niche. They are wrong, and the math tells you why:

03Know Your Customer Cold

If you cannot describe her day in 90 seconds, you cannot sell her.

The ICP: "Diana the Director"

Internalize this person so thoroughly that every product decision, every landing page, every cold email is written for her:

  • Name: Diana. Age 42–58.
  • Title: Owner/Director of a single-site child care center, 40–90 kids, 8–14 staff.
  • Background: Former teacher or director who bought or opened this facility 5–20 years ago. No MBA. No IT department. No compliance officer — she is the compliance officer.
  • Tools: Paper folders in a filing cabinet. A binder labeled "INSPECTION." QuickBooks, Gmail, maybe brightwheel for parent communication. Excel on a Dell laptop from 2019.
  • Income: She pays herself $60–110k. Her facility nets 6–12% margin. She knows to the dollar what $99/month means.
  • Decision authority: Total. She signs up in one sitting if she trusts you.
  • Trust pattern: Deeply suspicious of anything that looks like a tech bro pitch. Warm to other operators, consultants, and her state licensing specialist.
  • Biggest fear: A surprise inspection finds a missing record, she gets a citation, a parent hears about it, enrollment drops 20%, she cannot make payroll. This is not paranoia — it has happened to people she knows.
  • Trigger events: Failed inspection at her center or a nearby one. Staff turnover. New state rule. A horror story in a Facebook director group.
  • Where she hangs out: State director associations, Facebook groups ("FL Child Care Directors," "TX Daycare Owners"), annual state conferences, licensing workshops, her licensing specialist's emails.

The second persona: "Robert, the multi-site owner"

Shows up around year 2. 3–25 sites. Often a small regional chain or a franchise holder. Has an office manager or "director of operations" who spends 30 hours/week consolidating compliance across sites in spreadsheets. Robert has money, urgency, and will pay $199–399/site. You go from a $1,200/year customer to a $40,000/year customer in the same sales cycle. This is the lever that makes your business interesting. See Section 11.

The third persona (avoid for now): Franchise HQ

KinderCare, The Learning Experience, Primrose, Goddard. Great logos, 18-month sales cycles, will destroy you if you chase them before you have a product team. Route all inbound from franchise HQs to a waitlist until year 3. When they are ready, they pay like an enterprise SaaS deal: $100k–500k/year with security reviews and SSO requirements.

Rule: the one-sentence test

If any feature, headline, or pricing page section does not make sense to Diana as she reads it one-handed at 9:47pm after putting her kids to bed, cut it. You are not selling to a VP of IT.

04Product Philosophy

What to build. What not to build. How to decide.

The product in one sentence

ComplianceKit turns "I hope we're ready for inspection" into "We're inspection-ready every day, and I can prove it."

The three-circle product rule

Every feature must sit in at least two of these circles, or it gets cut:

1. State-specific

It does something a horizontal tool cannot. Florida's CF-FSP 5274 is not just a PDF — it is your moat. If brightwheel could add this feature in a sprint, it is not a moat.

2. Consequence-bearing

It prevents a citation, a fine, or a license issue. Features that only save time are nice. Features that prevent consequences are sold.

3. Recurring

It generates value every week, not just at onboarding. One-shot tools ("generate your handbook once") have zero retention. Staff certifications expire every year. Children turn 2 and need new forms.

The cutting floor

Parent messaging, billing, CRM, curriculum, lesson planning, time clock. All of these are real needs. All of them have better tools. Do not build them. Integrate (or ignore).

The MVP you ship by April 23

If something is not on this list, it does not ship this month:

  1. Compliance dashboard — a single score (0–100) and a 90-day deadline timeline. This is the screen they screenshot and text to their partner.
  2. Child file tracker — state-specific checklist per child. Immunization records, emergency forms, enrollment paperwork. Red/yellow/green status.
  3. Staff file tracker — certifications with expiration dates (CPR, First Aid, state training hours, background check renewal). Automatic 90/60/30-day alerts.
  4. Document upload + storage — parents upload immunization records via a magic-link portal. No account creation required on their end.
  5. Email notifications — 90/60/30/7-day alerts for every expiring item, per state rules.
  6. Billing — Stripe, three plans (see Pricing).
Not in MVP — resist the urge

SMS alerts. Mobile app. Drill scheduler. Wall posting tracker. Ratio calculator. Inspection simulator. Multi-site support. SSO. API. Custom branding. Parent messaging. Billing-for-parents. Staff time clock. Payroll. Curriculum. Learning domains. All of these are your roadmap — not your MVP. Ship the six things above, then talk to ten customers before you touch anything else.

Feature prioritization: the scoring rubric

For everything beyond MVP, score each candidate feature 1–5 on three axes:

  • Lose deal? Will a prospect walk without it? (5 = yes, common objection)
  • Retention impact? Does it create weekly/monthly usage? (5 = daily habit)
  • Moat? Does it deepen state-specific data or integration? (5 = hard to copy)

Build the highest scorers. Kill or defer anything that sums below 9. Track this in a single Airtable base. Re-score it every month. This is the first "process" you allow yourself to adopt; everything else stays on a napkin until year 2.

05UX Principles

Designing for a director at 9:47pm with three browser tabs and no patience.

The five UX laws of ComplianceKit

Law 1: Always answer "Am I OK?" in under 3 seconds

The dashboard is the app. Everything else supports it. When Diana opens ComplianceKit, the first thing she must see, above the fold, without scrolling, is a single answer: "You are 94/100. Three items need attention this week." Not a table. Not a chart library. A sentence, a score, and a short list. If the dashboard ever makes her work to figure out whether she's OK, you have failed at the one job.

Law 2: Every red item links to the exact fix

Status without remediation is cruelty. If a record is missing, the row says "Missing Form CF-FSP 5274" and clicking it jumps to a page that says here is the blank form, here is who needs to sign it, send it to them with one click. Not a help article. Not a modal with instructions. The fix, inline.

Law 3: Never ask for data you can infer

If Diana tells you her state is Florida and her facility type is "child care center," you already know which forms apply. Do not ask. Pre-fill relentlessly. When her parent uploads an immunization record, run OCR and pre-fill the dates. She confirms with one tap. The product should feel like it is doing her work, not asking her to feed it.

Law 4: Destructive actions require a receipt

"Delete child record" should be hard, not clever. Use a typed confirmation ("type DELETE") or a two-step modal. Every destructive action generates an audit log entry that is visible and immutable. Compliance users are trained to distrust software; you earn trust by making your software visibly careful.

Law 5: Write like a human, error like a guide

No "Error 400 Bad Request." No "Something went wrong." Errors say: "We couldn't save that — it looks like Sarah's CPR date is before her hire date. Click here to edit." Every error has a cause, a fix, and a link. Your error copy is a retention feature.

The six screens that do 90% of the work

  1. Dashboard (score + 90-day timeline + critical alerts)
  2. Children list (all kids, status chip, overdue count)
  3. Child detail (checklist of state-required docs, upload, parent portal link)
  4. Staff list (all staff, expiration chips, training hour progress)
  5. Staff detail (certifications, uploads, background check)
  6. Inspection-ready report (one-click PDF to print and hand to inspector)

If a screen is not one of these six, it is a settings screen or a side feature. Spend 80% of your design energy on these six.

Onboarding: the first 10 minutes

This is where most compliance SaaS fails. Don't ask for 45 fields. Ask for three:

  1. State (dropdown: FL, TX, CA for now)
  2. Facility type (center vs. family home)
  3. Facility name

Then generate a pre-populated compliance framework based on state rules. Show the dashboard immediately — even if it is empty, it is theirs. Every additional field moves to a "complete your profile" prompt that lives as a progressive reveal. The magic moment is when she sees her state's specific forms listed in the first 60 seconds. She'll think: "Finally, someone who actually knows what I need."

The 10-minute activation goal

By minute 10, Diana should have: (1) added one child, (2) added one staff member, (3) uploaded one document, (4) seen her compliance score change, (5) invited one parent to the portal. If she does all five, her 90-day retention triples. Measure this. Optimize for this. This single funnel is more important than your pricing page.

06UI & Visual Language

How the software should look. And why Diana should feel safe.

The aesthetic: institutional calm

This is not a consumer app. This is not a crypto dashboard. This is not a developer tool. Your visual language is closer to a bank, a law firm website, or a high-end electronic health record. Serious. Clean. Not flashy. Absolutely not playful. No emoji, no illustrations of cartoon babies, no rainbow gradients. Diana is trusting you with records that protect her license. The UI must look like it knows that.

The type system

  • One typeface. Inter, system-ui, or (if you pay the license) Söhne. Not multiple display fonts.
  • Four sizes. 12/14/16/24 px. Keep it boring. Hierarchy comes from weight and spacing, not from exotic sizes.
  • Two weights. Regular (450) and semibold (650). Avoid thin weights — they read as tech-bro.

The color system

  • Neutral-first. Slate/zinc grays, off-white background, dark ink for text.
  • One accent. A deep, slightly desaturated blue (#1e40af works). This is your brand.
  • Semantic colors. Red for alerts, amber for warnings, green for healthy. Use these only for status — never for decoration.
  • No gradients, no shadows deeper than a 2px soft drop. Flat, calm, readable.

The component vocabulary

Build a small, disciplined set and use it everywhere. Reusing eight components across the app is better than designing twenty bespoke ones:

  1. Status chip (red / amber / green + text)
  2. Card (paper background, 1px border, 10px radius)
  3. Table row (hover state, status chip inline, no zebra stripes)
  4. Button (primary, secondary, destructive — no ghost buttons)
  5. Input (label above, helper text below, error red + message)
  6. Modal (for confirmations and short forms only — avoid as navigation)
  7. Toast (success/error, auto-dismiss 4s)
  8. Timeline row (for the 90-day view)

Three UI moves that build trust

  1. Receipt language. After every save: "Saved. Last edited by you, 2 minutes ago." Show the user their own actions back to them.
  2. State-specific citations. When a field is required, show why: "Required by FL CFOP 170-20, §5.2.1." This is the unfakeable detail that signals to Diana that you did the work.
  3. Empty states that teach. An empty "Staff" screen doesn't say "No staff yet." It says "Add your first staff member. You'll need a copy of their CPR card, Level 2 background check, and at least 10 hours of completed training." This turns dead screens into onboarding.
The print test

Every "inspection-ready report" in the product must be designed as if it will be printed and handed to a state inspector. Because it will be. That means: letterhead with facility name, date, facility license number. Legible tables. Page numbers. No hyperlinks, no chips, no tooltips. Your print CSS is a P0 feature, not an afterthought.

07Engineering & Technical

A dev's operating manual for the next 18 months.

Architectural principles (the short list)

  1. Boring stack. Go + Postgres + a single-page React frontend. No microservices. No Kubernetes. One binary, one database, one Cloudflare/Fly region, one CDN. You can serve 10,000 customers from this before you need anything fancier.
  2. Monolith until painful. Extract services only when a specific team (that does not exist) needs to own one. Solo devs who "prepare for scale" with microservices are punishing themselves for a problem they don't have.
  3. Multi-tenant with a hard tenant_id. Every query, every row. No exceptions. Set up Postgres row-level security or enforce at the repository layer, and write a test that scans every table to ensure it has provider_id.
  4. Migrations are the product's memory. Numbered, reviewed, never edited after merge. Your migrations are your real schema doc.
  5. Logs > metrics > traces. Structured JSON logs from day one. Add metrics when you get paged. Add tracing when a request touches three services (never).
  6. Everything derives from state. A compliance score is a pure function of the documents on file. Treat derived state as a view, not a stored value. This saves you a category of bugs.

The directory structure (sketch)

backend/
  cmd/api/           # single binary entrypoint
  internal/
    handlers/        # HTTP layer — thin, no business logic
    domain/          # pure business logic — no DB, no HTTP
    db/              # repositories — SQL lives here
    compliance/      # the rules engine — state-specific requirements
    notify/          # email/SMS dispatch
    billing/         # stripe
    pdfsign/         # your e-sign module
  migrations/
frontend/
  src/
    routes/          # one file per page
    components/      # shared UI
    lib/             # api client, auth helpers

Your existing repo looks close to this. Keep it. Resist over-abstraction. A handler that is 120 lines of SQL-calling code is fine — do not extract it into a "service" until a second handler needs the same logic.

The compliance rules engine

This is the heart of the product. Design it as declarative rules, not imperative code. Each state becomes a directory of YAML or JSON files that describe:

  • What documents a child needs at what age
  • What certifications a staff role needs and their renewal windows
  • What drills run at what frequency
  • What postings must be on walls
  • What ratios apply at what age bands

Your Go code is a single evaluator that reads these rules and emits status. When a new state is added, you write rules and state-specific forms — no application code changes. This is the architecture that lets a solo founder add a state in a week.

The moat you're building

Every rule you encode is a capital expenditure that your competitors must also make to enter the market. By year 2 you will have encoded 10 states' rules; a new entrant needs to spend 6–12 months just to catch up. This is your defensibility. Take rule-encoding seriously — have each ruleset reviewed by a licensing specialist in that state (you can pay one $500 for a one-hour review).

Non-negotiable security basics (year 1)

  1. All traffic over HTTPS. Redirect HTTP to HTTPS at the edge.
  2. Hashed passwords (argon2id) OR magic-link only (preferred — fewer support tickets).
  3. Tenant isolation. Every SELECT/UPDATE/DELETE includes provider_id = $1. Write a linter that catches the one you'll forget at 2am.
  4. Encrypted at rest (Postgres + S3 defaults are fine).
  5. Daily backups to a separate region. Test the restore quarterly.
  6. Audit log. Every write to a compliance record is logged (who, what, when, from IP).
  7. Rate limit the API. Especially auth and upload endpoints.
  8. Secret management. 1Password or SOPS — not .env files in git. Ever.

What to skip in year 1 (and add on demand)

  • SOC 2. Needed for enterprise deals, not for Diana. When your first enterprise prospect asks, you have ~90 days; start the audit then, not now.
  • SSO / SAML. Same. Implement when an enterprise customer makes it a blocker.
  • HIPAA. You are not a health provider. Immunization records are sensitive but not PHI under the HIPAA scope. Talk to a lawyer for your state, but default to "no, don't claim HIPAA."
  • A mobile app. A PWA with an "Add to Home Screen" prompt solves 95% of this. Real mobile apps are an ongoing tax you can't afford solo.
  • Real-time anything. Compliance is not a chat app. Stale-while-revalidate is fine.

How to spend your engineering time

60%

Customer-visible features

New screens, new integrations, new state support. If Diana cannot see it, it is not customer-visible.

20%

Bug fixes and polish

Real customer pain, not theoretical concerns. Fix what comes through support tickets that week.

15%

Tooling and infra

Deploys, testing, observability. Enough to be fast, not enough to be beautiful. If you spend Saturday on Kubernetes, this is wrong.

5%

Refactor

Only when the code is actively blocking you from shipping a feature. Do not "clean up" — you'll rewrite it anyway.

08Data, Compliance Engine, Moat

What makes you hard to copy.

Four layers of defensibility

Layer 1 — Regulation encoding

Thousands of state-specific rules, maintained as they change. The labor to produce this is real. Competitors cannot shortcut it with AI — the penalty for being wrong is a customer's license.

Layer 2 — Customer data

Once a center has a year of records in ComplianceKit, switching costs are high — not because of lock-in tricks, but because re-entry is painful. Design for export, but switching still hurts. This is a healthy moat.

Layer 3 — Integrations

Once you integrate with state licensing portals (FL Child Care Licensing, TX CCL, CA CCLD), competitors must repeat the integration, which can take months. Start this in year 2.

Layer 4 — Brand + distribution

When Diana's licensing specialist says "I've seen ComplianceKit and it's good," you've won. Invest in the specialists, the consultants, the state associations — they are your distribution.

Data you should be collecting (privately, for yourself)

  • Activation funnel. Signup → first child added → first staff added → first document uploaded → 7-day retention.
  • Score movement. How does compliance score change in the first 30 days? This predicts churn more reliably than any survey.
  • Feature touch frequency. Which screens get opened weekly? Those are your core loops. Screens no one opens are candidates to cut.
  • Churn cause. Every cancellation asks one question: "What happened?" Categorize with your own eyes for the first 100 churns. Do not outsource this.
The sleeping data product

By year 3, you will have aggregate data about compliance health across thousands of facilities. This data is valuable to states themselves — licensing agencies would love to know that centers using ComplianceKit have 40% fewer citations. It is also valuable to insurance carriers (lower premiums for demonstrably compliant facilities) and funders (child care subsidy compliance reporting). Do not pursue this before year 3 — but build the data hygiene now so it's possible later.

Most founders under-price. Do not be one of them.

The three-tier launch

Pricing principles

  1. Charge monthly by default, offer 2 months free for annual. Monthly lowers the commitment barrier for SMBs. Annual is a retention mechanism, not a discount.
  2. Do not offer a free tier. A free tier attracts the wrong users and increases support load. Offer a 14-day free trial instead, no credit card required.
  3. Do not offer per-child pricing. It punishes growing centers and invites comparison shopping. Per-site is the right unit.
  4. Raise prices every 12 months for new customers. Grandfather existing ones for a year, then migrate them with 60-day notice and a letter.
  5. Publish prices. "Contact us for pricing" signals you are afraid of the number. You are not.

When to raise prices

You are too cheap when:

  • Customers sign up in under 5 minutes with zero objections. A small amount of price pushback is healthy.
  • Your win rate exceeds 60% in discovery calls.
  • Your LTV/CAC ratio is above 8 (anything >5 is fine, >8 means you can reinvest more).
  • You have raised your average invoice 0 times in 18 months.

Default cadence: raise 15–20% in year 2, another 15% in year 3. By year 4, Professional should be $149–179/mo. Almost no one churns over a price change if the product still works.

Discounts: the discipline

Give up to 20% for annual. Give up to 10% for a published referral. Never discount ad hoc. Discounts should be a list Diana can point at, not a negotiation. Exception: enterprise contracts can be negotiated, but only against volume and commitment length, not against your base rate. "You can get 15% off for 5+ sites annual prepaid" is a policy. "I'll give you 15% because you pushed back" is a leak.

10Marketing — SEO-Led Growth

You cannot out-spend anyone. You can out-content them.

The strategy in one paragraph

Your entire marketing plan is: own every query a daycare operator types when she is worried about compliance. These queries are high-intent, low-volume, and under-served — exactly the opposite of what a VC-backed horizontal would chase. You already have the head start: state-specific inspection guides, immunization guides, and startup guides. Keep going. By year 2 you should own 2,000+ long-tail queries.

The content engine

Weekly

2 long-form articles (~2,500 words each)

State-specific. Answer a specific licensing question or walk through a specific form. Internal link to related guides. End with a product CTA that is subtle, not a banner ad.

Monthly

1 "pillar page" per state

A comprehensive, updated-quarterly resource. Ex: "Florida Daycare Compliance: Everything You Need to Know in 2026." This is the page that ranks for head terms and funnels into the rest.

Quarterly

1 benchmark or report

"The 2026 State of Child Care Compliance" — pulled from your own (aggregated, anonymized) customer data once you have enough. These are link magnets.

The four content formats that actually convert

  1. "How to pass inspection in [state]" — you have this. Keep updating annually.
  2. "How to start a daycare in [state]" — reaches pre-launch operators who will become customers on day one of their license.
  3. "[State] form [number] explained" — ultra-long-tail, low competition, high intent (they are filling it out right now).
  4. "Checklist / printable / template" — email-gated downloads. Your primary top-of-funnel.

Distribution: beyond SEO

  • Facebook groups. Join every state director group. Answer questions. Never link your product unsolicited. Occasionally reference ComplianceKit when directly asked. The community will do the rest.
  • Licensing specialists. Identify the state-employed specialists who visit centers. Meet one per state. Offer a free account. They become evangelists — not formally, but in conversation.
  • Consultants. Child care compliance consultants exist in every state. Build a referral program: 30% of year-one revenue on referrals they close. Some will love you; one will quietly send you 10 customers a quarter.
  • State associations. FCCDAA, TLA, CCDAA, etc. Sponsor a modest booth at the annual conference in your top state. Do one per year. Meet 50 directors in person. Close 5. That's $6,000/year in new ARR from one weekend, plus ten relationships that feed referrals.
  • Podcast appearances. There are ~12 child care operator podcasts. Be on all of them in year 2.

What not to spend money on (year 1)

  • Paid ads. Your CAC blows up and you learn nothing about your customer. Earn the right to pay for distribution; don't shortcut to it.
  • A rebrand. Your logo is fine. Your website is fine. Nobody has ever churned because of a logo.
  • Billboards, trade show sponsorships at $20k+. Save these for year 3.
  • "Thought leadership" on LinkedIn. Your customers aren't there.
  • A podcast of your own. Don't start one in year 1. Content machine you cannot feed.
The one marketing metric that matters

Qualified trial starts per week. Not traffic. Not impressions. Not email opens. A qualified trial is someone who signed up from your site, entered a real facility name, and added at least one child. Everything upstream is a means to grow this one number. If the number is flat for three weeks, the question isn't "how do I make more content"; it's "why did the last 40 trials not convert." Watch session replays. Talk to them. Fix the funnel.

11Sales — Founder as First Rep

For the first 100 customers, you are the entire sales team. That is a good thing.

The founder-sales theorem

The most valuable sales activity in the first 12 months is you on the phone. Not because you're a trained rep (you aren't), but because:

  • You hear the objections unfiltered and can ship fixes the same day.
  • Every objection is a content idea, a feature idea, and a pricing data point.
  • Customers trust founders more than reps in this market.
  • You learn the language — the words Diana uses — which makes all your copy better.

Do this for at least 100 conversations. Do not hire a salesperson before this. You will regret it for three reasons: they won't know the domain, they can't fix the product, and they will pipeline fill with bad-fit accounts to hit a quota.

The inbound conversation flow

A director fills out a demo form. You have 15 minutes to run this call. Here's the shape:

0:00–1:00

Open with their world, not yours

"Hey Diana — thanks for taking the time. Before I show you anything, can I just ask — what made you fill out the form today? Was there a specific thing you were looking to solve?"

1:00–5:00

Discovery — ask, don't pitch

Goals: what state, how many kids, how many staff, how do they currently track compliance, when was their last inspection, what went wrong. Listen for the emotional trigger — almost every signup has one. Write it down.

5:00–11:00

Demo the one thing that matches

Do not run the full product tour. Show the dashboard, show the one feature that hits her trigger, show a state-specific form. Total of three screens. Stop talking.

11:00–14:00

Price and close

"Our Professional plan is $99/month. First 14 days free, no card required. Would you like me to set you up right now, or would you like me to send you a link?" Shut up. The first one to speak loses. 90% say "right now" if you did discovery correctly.

14:00–15:00

If she signs up: "Great — I'll stay on for 2 more minutes to get you through your first kid file. That's where everyone gets stuck." If she didn't: "What would make this an easy yes for you?" Listen. This is your best feedback in the world.

How to find them

  1. From your own funnel. When someone signs up, look at their facility count. Anyone with 3+ sites is an upsell candidate — route them to you personally.

The enterprise sales motion — step by step

Step 1 — The warm-up (week 1)

Never pitch Robert the first time you meet him. Ask about his business. How many sites, what states, what's his hardest operational problem. Listen for the word "compliance" in his top three issues. If it's not there, don't push — he's not ready.

Step 2 — The hook (weeks 1–2)

Robert is not Diana. His pain isn't personal panic — it's leverage. He can't see across his sites. One location's director is great, another is a liability, and he finds out only when a citation lands. Your pitch:

Pitch

"Right now you're running 8 compliance programs, not one. You trust each director, but you don't have the reporting to know which site is drifting until an inspector tells you. We give you a single dashboard across all your sites — you see, in real time, which site is at risk this week, which staff certification is about to expire, and where you need to intervene. Your directors keep running their sites; you stop running blind."

Step 3 — The pilot (weeks 2–6)

Do not negotiate a 10-site contract on day one. Offer this:

The pilot playbook

"Let's run a 60-day paid pilot at 2 of your sites." Price it at list — $99/site/mo for 2 months, so $396 total. This is important: you want the pilot to convert, not to prove a point. Low price, not free, so Robert has skin in the game. Agree in writing on 3 success criteria:

  1. Both sites onboarded in 10 days.
  2. Compliance score for both sites above 90 by day 45.
  3. Robert can generate a consolidated report in under 5 minutes.

If you hit the criteria, the pilot converts to an annual contract at all sites. This framing does two things: it makes the rollout risk-free for Robert, and it turns you from a vendor into a partner who sets measurable goals with him.

Step 4 — The expansion close (weeks 6–10)

Pilot ends, criteria met. Now the negotiation. Do not discount your base rate. Instead, structure the value:

The contract itself

Use a boring MSA + order form. Do not invent a custom contract. Common terms to agree on up front:

  • Term: 12 months auto-renewing, 30-day notice to non-renew.
  • Payment: Annual prepaid; net 30 for billed annual.
  • Data ownership: Their data is theirs. You have a limited license to operate the service. On termination, they get a full export within 30 days.
  • Liability cap: Annual fees paid. Do not agree to uncapped liability for a $40k/year deal.
  • SLA (if asked): 99.5% uptime, measured monthly. Credits capped at one month's fees. Don't offer 99.9% until you're ready to be woken up.
  • Indemnity: You indemnify for your IP infringement; they indemnify for their use of the product and their data. Mutual carve-outs, standard.
  • Termination for convenience: Not allowed mid-term. Period. If they push, allow a one-time exit with 60-day notice and forfeit of remaining fees (they won't take it, but it feels fair).
Redlines to push back on — hard

When Robert's lawyer sends back redlines, the ones to refuse or fight:

  • Uncapped liability. Never. Counter with: annual fees paid, carve-outs for confidentiality and IP infringement only.
  • Custom security questionnaire as contract term. Your SOC 2 (when you have it) or security summary is the answer. Don't sign up to a 200-question annual audit for a $40k deal.
  • Exclusivity / MFN pricing. Never agree to give them the best price ever. You will raise prices, and this clause punishes you forever.
  • Uptime >= 99.9%. Counter with 99.5% until you have real infrastructure.
  • Source code escrow. Fine — agree, and never fund it. It's a $200/yr deposit with a service.
  • Unlimited users / sites. "All current and future sites" is a pricing time bomb. Price per site, always.

The enterprise playbook in one page

  1. Never discount your base rate. Volume tiers are policy.
  2. Always price per site, not per customer. Growth is your customer's; not your gift.
  3. Pilot first, annual second. Skin in the game from both sides.
  4. Procurement will ask for 20 things. 17 are templates. Say yes. The other 3 require your judgment — fight those.
  5. Every "no" needs a "but here's what I can do." Never end on a hard no.
  6. Slow-walk legal. Enterprise deals close fastest when you set the timeline. "I have availability to close this contract in the next 2 weeks; after that our pricing increases on April 1." Urgency is your friend.
  7. Treat the CSM like a line item. Robert wants a human to email. Give him one (you, at first). Put it in the contract as "dedicated customer success contact, monthly check-in." It costs you an email and closes the deal.
  8. Renewal starts on day 1. Month 10 is too late. Send Robert a monthly "here's the value you got this month" email starting month 1. It feels paranoid. It raises renewal rate 20%.

Save motions — when they try to cancel

Every cancellation attempt routes to you (at first) with a small form:

  • Which of these best describes why: (a) closing our facility, (b) switching tools, (c) too expensive, (d) not using it enough, (e) other?

Response by reason:

  • Closing the facility: "I'm sorry. Keep the account free for 3 months in case it's temporary, or export your data — here's the link." Wins goodwill, sometimes the facility reopens.
  • Switching tools: "I'd love to understand what we're missing. Can I send you a 15-minute Calendly?" Half won't talk. The other half will tell you something critical.
  • Too expensive: Offer the Starter plan, not a discount on Professional. Discounts on Professional teach the market to negotiate.
  • Not using it enough: Your onboarding failed. Offer 1 free month + a personal onboarding call. Recover 40% this way.

The expansion motion

Every time a customer's facility count, staff count, or child count changes upward, they become an upsell candidate. Triggers to watch:

  • Added a second location's data → Enterprise pilot pitch.
  • Hit the Starter plan's soft cap → Professional upgrade pitch.
  • Hired a new compliance lead / director → offer a 15-min onboarding for the new person (strengthens the account).
  • Passed an inspection with a perfect report generated from your software → ask for a testimonial. 1 in 4 says yes.

Runway math

You are bootstrapping. Your personal burn + business burn is the number that matters. Write this down:

  • Monthly personal expenses: $____ (rent/mortgage, food, insurance, etc.)
  • Monthly business expenses: ~$150 (tools) + ~$50 (domain, legal, accounting)
  • Cash on hand: $____
  • Months of runway at current burn: cash / (personal + business burn)

Default-alive when MRR covers personal + business burn. Celebrate that day — it's the most important milestone in your business.

To raise, or not to raise

Reasons to stay bootstrapped

  • You keep 100% of the company.
  • You decide when to stop.
  • Churn this vertical is low and cash-flow is strong — you don't need capital to compound.
  • No board asking "what's the exit plan" quarterly.
  • $5M ARR at 80% GM = $4M/yr cash, yours. Nobody can tell you what to do.

Reasons to raise (carefully)

  • You have validated the model ($1M+ ARR, <2% monthly churn) and want to accelerate.
  • A competitor just raised and is outspending you in paid acquisition (but this rarely works in vertical SaaS).
  • You want to hire aggressively and not wait for cash flow.
  • An enterprise deal requires SOC 2 / security investment you can't afford.

My recommendation: stay bootstrapped through $1M ARR. Raise only if a specific, concrete opportunity requires capital you can't generate from cash flow. A "just in case" round is the worst round. It kills discipline, dilutes you, and introduces pressure that changes your decision-making.

The one-page P&L

Even solo, run a simple P&L every month. Xero, Wave, or a Google Sheet. Track:

  • MRR (by plan)
  • New MRR / expansion MRR / churned MRR / net new
  • Stripe fees, hosting, tools (COGS)
  • Marketing / sales spend (even if it's just content tools)
  • Your "salary" (even if it's $0 — this matters for equity valuations later)
  • Cash in bank (separate from burn — this is your survival metric)

Legal / tax housekeeping (do once, forget)

  • LLC taxed as S-corp once revenue clears ~$80k/yr (saves self-employment tax). A CPA can do this in a day for ~$500.
  • Separate business bank account from day one. Do not co-mingle.
  • Quarterly estimated taxes. Set aside 30% of profit in a "tax" account. Pay quarterly.
  • State sales tax on SaaS: varies by state. Hire a CPA familiar with multi-state SaaS by the time you pass $250k ARR.
  • Terms of Service + Privacy Policy + DPA. Use Termly or Common Paper templates. Ask a lawyer for a 2-hour review before your first enterprise deal.
  • Cyber liability insurance: $500–1500/yr once you hit real revenue. Required by enterprise customers anyway.

16Hiring — The First Five

Hire later than you think you should. Then hire better than you think you can.

The order

  1. Virtual assistant (month ~6, ~$1–2k/mo). Philippines or LatAm. Inbox triage, basic support ticket triage, invoice chasing, CRM hygiene. Buys you back 15 hours a week.
  2. Customer success manager #1 (month ~12–15, ~$60–75k salary). Ideally a former child care director who knows the customer's world. She (likely she) onboards new customers, runs QBRs, saves churn. Your single highest-leverage hire.
  3. Content marketer / SEO (month ~15–18, ~$60–80k or $4k/mo contractor). Takes over the content engine. Former journalist or education-vertical writer beats an "SEO expert." They have to care about the domain.
  4. Second engineer (month ~18–24, ~$120–160k). Senior generalist, ships end-to-end, comfortable being the only dev pair. No specialists until year 3. Equity-heavy if bootstrapping.
  5. Sales rep / AE (month ~24–30). You have run 200+ founder sales calls. You have a documented playbook. Hire someone who has sold to SMBs in regulated verticals (healthcare, food service, education). Pay 50/50 base/commission on a $100k OTE.
What not to hire in year 1–2
  • A "COO" or "head of ops." You don't have enough ops for one person.
  • A "head of marketing" with an agency background. They will recommend agencies. You don't need agencies.
  • A "head of product." You are the head of product. Until you are drowning in it.
  • A "BD" or "partnerships" person. Nothing to partner on yet.
  • An offshore dev shop. You will spend more time reviewing their work than doing it yourself.

How to hire well when you're solo

  1. Write a real JD. Not "we're looking for a passionate." Describe the first 90 days in specifics. What they'll ship, who they'll talk to, what success looks like at day 30 and day 90.
  2. Source from your customers. The single best CSM hire you can make is a former ComplianceKit customer who loved the product. They already speak the language.
  3. Three interviews, max. (1) Phone screen, (2) paid trial task (4–8 hours, paid at their rate), (3) culture/trust conversation with you. Do not run 8-round processes.
  4. Reference checks are mandatory. Two references you pick, not just theirs. Ask "would you hire them again, knowing what you know now?" The pause before the answer tells you everything.
  5. 90-day trial period. Be generous if it doesn't work — 1 month severance, good reference. Firing slow is the most expensive mistake a solo founder can make.

17Legal, Trust, Risk

Boring, unavoidable, and the reason some deals close and others don't.

The documents you need in year 1

  1. Terms of Service — Termly or Common Paper template.
  2. Privacy Policy — CCPA + FTC compliant. Say what you collect, why, and how to delete.
  3. DPA (Data Processing Addendum) — enterprise customers will ask; have a template ready.
  4. Subprocessor list — a public URL listing AWS/Cloudflare/Stripe/Postmark etc. Updated when you change vendors.
  5. Security summary — one-page PDF with your security posture (encryption, auth, backups, audit logs). Updated quarterly.
  6. Customer agreement / MSA — for annual and enterprise. Template from Common Paper is 85% done.

The SOC 2 decision

You do not need SOC 2 for year 1. You will need it for enterprise. When an enterprise prospect asks "are you SOC 2?", the right answers in order:

  • Year 1: "Not yet — we'll start the audit when we have a deal that requires it. Meanwhile, here's our security summary."
  • Year 2: "We're SOC 2 Type 1, Type 2 audit in progress." (Type 1 is cheap and fast; Type 2 requires 6 months of evidence collection.)
  • Year 3: "SOC 2 Type 2, refreshed annually." (Non-negotiable for enterprise by now.)

Use Vanta, Drata, or Secureframe — they cost ~$7k–15k/yr and compress the SOC 2 audit from a 9-month nightmare into a ~3-month project. Do not try to DIY SOC 2. It is the single most leveraged third-party tool in the SaaS stack.

18The Mental Game

The technical parts are the easy parts. This is the real work.

You will want to quit. Not once — many times. Tuesday at 11pm when the third customer in a row says "I'll think about it." The week MRR goes down because of a weird churn cluster. The month your personal bank account has less in it than last month. The morning you see a LinkedIn post from someone you went to college with who just raised a Series B. These moments are universal and they do not mean anything about your business. They mean you are in the trough.

"The only people who don't quit are the people who stopped expecting the feeling of quitting to mean something. You feel like quitting. You don't quit. You keep going. That is all. Nobody who built anything was spared the feeling." — Every founder who shipped

The rules of the mental game

1. Never decide anything emotional late at night.

Whether to pivot, whether to quit, whether to accept that one discount, whether to email that customer back angrily — none of these get decided after 9pm. Write it down, sleep, decide in the morning. You will be a different person by 8am. This single rule will save you twenty stupid decisions a year.

2. Loneliness is an operational risk. Solve it.

Solo founding is lonely in a way that hurts your decision-making. You need two things:

  • A weekly peer call. One or two other solo founders at your stage. Honest. No bullshit. An hour on Zoom, once a week. You'll spend it complaining, laughing, and realizing none of your problems are unique.
  • A monthly mentor call. Someone 5–10 years ahead of you in vertical SaaS. They don't need to be famous. They need to have sold a $1M+ ARR bootstrapped company. One hour a month. Pay them if needed.

3. Celebrate the small wins, out loud, to someone.

When you close a new customer, text someone. When you hit $10k MRR, make dinner plans. When a customer says "this saved my license" — take a screenshot, print it, put it on the wall. You will need these artifacts on the days when the spreadsheet is lying to you about how bad things are.

4. Keep something that is not the company.

A hobby, a sport, a non-tech friendship, a weekend ritual. Something that exists whether or not the company does. Founders who are their company break when the company is breaking. Founders who have a life with the company in it have leverage the other kind never has.

5. The compounding thing works.

In year 1, you will feel like nothing is happening. Traffic is flat. MRR is flat. You'll ship 40 features and think "why isn't anyone noticing." In year 2, things accelerate. In year 3, you will look back and not believe you ever worried about whether it would work. The only job in year 1 is to not stop. Ship. Talk to customers. Write. Sleep. Repeat. Boringly. Every week. For a year. The compounding does the rest.

6. When in doubt, talk to a customer.

Every existential doubt a founder has is answered by a 20-minute conversation with a real customer using the product today. If you feel lost, pick up the phone and call Diana. Ask her "how's it going?" She will tell you that the thing you built for her is helping her sleep at night. You will remember why you are doing this. This is a repeatable move. Use it.

The two questions to ask yourself every quarter

  1. Am I still excited to work on this on Monday morning? (If yes, keep going.)
  2. Are customers getting more value this quarter than last quarter? (If yes, the business is healthy, regardless of what the revenue line says.)

If both answers are yes, you are winning. The revenue catches up eventually. I promise.

The reassurance, straight up

You are building something that matters. Children are safer in child care centers that have their compliance in order. Directors sleep better. Parents trust the facility more. Staff stay longer because the paperwork doesn't eat their week. You are solving a real problem, for real people, in a category where nobody with your skill set is paying attention.

You have a technical superpower. You have a customer you understand. You have a market that is large, fragmented, and underserved. You have a four-year runway of content and state-specific knowledge already built. You have AI tooling that makes you 3x faster than a founder from 2020. The only way to lose is to stop.

You are not going to stop.

  3. Goal: 10 paying customers at $99/mo. Even if you have to onboard each one personally. Especially if you have to.
  4. Every signup gets a personal email from you within 2 hours.
  5. Every one of those 10 customers gets a 30-minute call in week 3 or 4. Learn.
  6. Log every objection, every feature request, every support ticket in a single doc.

Days 31–60 — Find the repeatable playbook

  1. Review the call log and the ticket log. Find the patterns.
  2. Ship the top 2–3 features customers actually asked for (not what you think is cool).
  3. Write 4 new long-form articles — one per week. Target long-tail state queries.
  4. Build the one-page state inspection PDF for FL, TX, CA. This is your sales closer.
  5. Goal: 25 paying customers. MRR ~$2.5k. Inbound trial starts ~15/week.
  6. Listen to all your sales call recordings on 1.75x. Rewrite your pitch.

Days 61–90 — Prove the motion, set up compounding

  1. Identify your 2 highest-converting marketing channels. Double down.
  2. Hire a VA for inbox + support triage. 15 hours/week, $300–500/mo.
  3. Ship a "parent upload portal" with magic-link — highest-leverage retention feature.
  4. Start identifying multi-site operators in your customer base. Pitch pilot to one.
  5. Goal: 50 paying customers. MRR ~$5k. First multi-site pilot in the pipe.
  6. Write the "Year 1 plan" document. Schedule the next 90-day planning session.
What "success" looks like at day 90

50 paying customers. $5,000 MRR. Organic traffic trending up. A one-page pitch that closes 30%+ of demo calls. A product that you haven't touched half the screens of in 2 weeks because they just work. One multi-site pilot conversation active. A weekly rhythm you can sustain. And, most importantly: you still want to work on this on Monday morning.

20A Closing Word

This document is not the truth. It is a starting point. You are going to discover things it gets wrong. You will find a better enterprise negotiation move in month 18 that makes this page look naive. You will find that your ideal customer is actually 60 kids, not 75. You will find that Texas is easier than Florida. You will find that one of the "Do Not" rules in here was exactly the right move in your specific situation. Good. That is how businesses get built. The document evolves with you.

What will not change is the core: understand Diana better than anyone. Build the product she needs, not the one you think is cool. Talk to her every week. Ship every week. Write every week. Do it for four years. You will be running a company that 30,000 child care operators across America depend on to keep their doors open.
