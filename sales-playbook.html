1. The mindset problem (read this first)

You said you're unsure about cold calling. That's the most important thing to solve. Everything else is mechanics — and mechanics you can learn in a weekend. Mindset takes longer and matters more.

What you are actually afraid of

It isn't the phone. It's the imagined rejection: the stranger dismissing you, sighing, being rude, telling you no. Your brain treats that as a social threat and floods you with avoidance. This is normal and near-universal among founders. It is also mostly fiction.

Real cold calls — even bad ones — almost never go how your anxiety predicts. The typical outcome is not cruelty. It's indifference. The person says "not interested, thanks" and hangs up. That's it. Indifference is survivable. Indifference is information.

Reframe

You are not selling. You are searching. Out of 100 daycare owners, some fraction are in acute compliance pain right now — an inspection coming, a violation they're sweating, a staff file they can't find. Your job isn't to convince anyone. Your job is to find the ones who are already looking for the thing you built.

Three mindset shifts that change everything

  1. Your product solves a real, expensive problem. A failed inspection is $500–$5,000 in fines and potential license suspension. You're offering $99/mo insurance. Calling someone to offer this is not rude. It's useful. If you genuinely believe that, the call changes.
  2. "No" is a feature, not a bug. Every "no" gets you closer to the next "yes" and frees a slot in your pipeline. Fast nos are valuable. Slow maybes are the enemy.
  3. The call is not a performance. It's a conversation between two business owners. You're not a telemarketer reading a script at a call center. You built the thing. You know the regulations better than they do. You are the expert on the other end of this line.

The two-week desensitization

You don't talk yourself out of call anxiety. You call your way out of it. The hack: set a low-stakes quota that is impossible to fail at — 10 dials/day for 10 business days — and only track dials, not outcomes. After ~50 dials, the physical dread drops dramatically. After ~100, the phone is just a phone.

Commit

Block 45 minutes tomorrow morning, 10:00–10:45 AM local time for the prospect. Pick 10 phone numbers from the FL DCF provider database the night before. Dial all 10. That's the whole assignment. You will book 0–2 demos from that first block. By block 10, you'll book 1–3 per block consistently.

3. Wearing three hats: AE, CSM, Support

Until you hire, you are the entire go-to-market org. The three roles have different jobs, different KPIs, and different headspaces. Learn to switch between them deliberately — context-switching without awareness is how founder-led sales produces burnout and dropped balls.

Founder anti-pattern

The #1 mistake solo founders make: drowning in support tickets at the expense of new-logo acquisition. Support feels urgent (it is) and productive (it is) but you cannot grow a company on support tickets. Protect your morning sales block ruthlessly. Batch support in 3 short sweeps: 8am, 1pm, 5pm.

4. The Account Executive hat

Your job here: move prospects from "never heard of us" to "paying $99/mo" as efficiently as possible without over-promising. In founder-led sales, that means discovery-heavy, honesty-heavy, and fast.

The pipeline stages you should track

  1. Prospect — in the ICP list, never contacted.
  2. Attempted — dialed or emailed, no response yet.
  3. Connected — had a real conversation (even a 60-second one).
  4. Demo booked — calendar invite accepted.
  5. Demo completed — showed the product.
  6. Trial / pilot — using the product.
  7. Proposal / verbal yes — money on the table, paperwork pending.
  8. Closed won / closed lost — terminal.

The sales cycle for a $99/mo daycare SaaS

Expect 3–21 days from first contact to paid. Fast closes (same week) happen when the prospect has an active inspection fear. Slow closes (weeks) happen when they're interested but not in pain. Don't force a slow deal. Drop it to nurture and move on.

The founder's unfair advantage

You are the founder. That matters. Lead with it. "I'm Magnus, I built ComplianceKit" is a more compelling opener than any hired SDR can deliver. Owners want to talk to the person who built the thing. Use that currency — but don't squander it on prospects who aren't in the ICP.

5. The CSM hat

At $99/mo, you cannot afford white-glove CSM per account. But you also can't afford churn — LTV math breaks instantly if churn goes above 10%/year. The trick: automated touchpoints with human escalation on signal.

The CSM motion, structured by account age

Day 0–7: Activation

  • Personal welcome email from you (not automated feel) within 1 hour of signup.
  • Day 1: 15-min onboarding call offered (not required). Use Calendly.
  • Day 3: Check that the account has uploaded at least one staff file and one child file. If not, trigger an in-app nudge + email.
  • Day 7: If compliance score is still 0 or below 40, call the owner directly.

Goal: activation = compliance score > 60 and at least one deadline tracked.

Day 8–30: Habit formation

  • Weekly digest email: "Your compliance score this week: X. Upcoming deadlines: Y."
  • Monitor: Has the owner logged in in the last 7 days? If not, yellow flag.
  • Day 30: 10-minute check-in call. Ask: "What's working? What's missing?"

Goal: the owner logs in at least 2x/week and has invited at least 1 staff member.

Day 31+: Retention & expansion

  • Quarterly QBR — 20 minutes, optional, positioned as free. Review compliance trend, upcoming inspections.
  • Track NPS at 60 days and again at 180 days.
  • Any customer with 9–10 NPS: ask for a referral + a public review.
  • Any customer scoring 0–6 NPS: immediate call from you. No email. Call.

Early churn signals (call within 24 hours of any of these)

  • No login for 14+ days.
  • Compliance score dropping 15+ points in a week.
  • Support ticket with the word "cancel," "refund," or "switch."
  • Failed payment.
  • Owner replies to a weekly digest with any negative sentiment.
  • A staff member at the account becomes inactive.

6. The Support hat

Support is where trust is built or lost. At $99/mo you cannot be 24/7. You can be fast, honest, and personal.

The founder-led support SLA (be explicit on the site)

  • Business hours: Mon–Fri, 8am–6pm PT.
  • First response: under 2 hours, business hours; under 12 hours otherwise.
  • Resolution target: same business day for P1 (down, data loss, inspection tomorrow). 3 business days for everything else.

Triage rules

P1 — Data loss / outageFiles missing, login broken, billing charged in errorReply within 1 hour, resolve within 4

Feature requests — how to respond without committing

Never say "yes we'll build that." Never say "no we won't." Say: "That's a really useful signal — let me add it to the roadmap. What's the exact scenario where this would save you time?" Then log it. When 3+ customers request the same thing, it becomes a real candidate.

7. Building the call list (free, public data)

Good lists beat good scripts. You do not need to pay for a prospect database — every state publishes licensed provider directories with names, addresses, and phone numbers.

State licensing directories (public, free)

  • Florida: DCF Provider Search — searchable by county, with phone numbers and capacity for every licensed facility.
  • Texas: HHSC Child Care Search — includes phone, address, type, and most recent inspection outcomes.
  • California: CDSS Community Care Licensing — provider lookup, downloadable facility lists.

Tier your list

Tier A (call first)

  • Recent violation on public record
  • Due for re-inspection in next 60 days
  • 30–75 children capacity

Tier B (call second)

  • No recent inspection signal

Always call Tier A first. They are in pain. They pick up. They convert.

9. Pre-call prep (5 minutes, every time)

Cold calling doesn't mean uninformed calling. Five minutes of research lets you open with something specific that signals you're not a telemarketer.

What to look up before dialing

  1. Owner's name. From state directory or facility website.
  2. Facility website. 30 seconds scanning home page: tone, claimed accreditations, staff count.
  3. Google reviews. 1 positive + 1 critical review if they exist. Gives you conversational ammunition.
  4. Recent inspection outcome. Public in all three states. If they had violations, you already know their pain.
  5. Regulatory deadlines relevant to their state right now. E.g., FL Director Credential renewals, TX training-hour cycles.

The 30-second pre-call ritual (physical)

  • Stand up. Voice sounds more energetic standing than sitting.
  • Smile before dialing. It's audible.
  • Have a glass of water in reach.
  • Speaker volume loud enough that you're not straining to hear.
  • Close Slack, email, all notifications. Single-task.
  • Open the prospect's row in your sheet so you can type as they talk.

10. The opening 15 seconds

You have roughly 15 seconds from "hello" to hook. If you don't get a micro-commitment in that window, they hang up. This is the single most important part of the call to rehearse.

The structure

  1. Name + who you are — 3 seconds
  2. Pattern interrupt / honesty — 3 seconds
  3. Reason for the call, specific to them — 5 seconds
  4. Permission-based micro-ask — 4 seconds

The opening — primary version

ThemSunshine Kids Academy, this is Maria.
YouHey Maria — this is Magnus, I'm the founder of a small software company called ComplianceKit. I'll be honest, this is a cold call. Do you have 27 seconds for me to tell you why I called, and then you can tell me to go away?
(pause — wait for them)

Why this opening works

  • "I'll be honest, this is a cold call" — pattern interrupt. They expect you to pretend it's not. Honesty disarms.
  • "27 seconds" — weirdly specific numbers signal you've thought about it. 30 sounds rehearsed; 27 sounds real.
  • "Then you can tell me to go away" — explicit permission to say no. This is judo. It removes the tension that makes them say no reflexively.
  • "I'm the founder" — signals this isn't a call center grunt. Owners respect owners.

The next 27 seconds (earn the meeting)

YouI built this because my [sister / cousin / neighbor — pick one, it's close enough to true since it's your market research] runs a daycare in [state], and she was up at 2 AM the night before her DCF inspection trying to find immunization records in a binder. ComplianceKit is basically an inspection-ready-every-day dashboard — tracks every state requirement for FL, TX, and CA, flags expiring staff certs and child files 90 days out, and gives you a simulated inspection you can run whenever you want.
YouI noticed on the DCF site you're coming up on your annual inspection in about [X] days. I don't know if you're already set, but if there's any chance it'd be useful to see the tool for 15 minutes, I'd love to show you. Worth a quick look?

The openings I'd have you memorize cold

Variant A — Direct honesty

"Hey Maria, this is Magnus. I'm the founder of ComplianceKit. I'm going to be straight with you — this is a cold call. Got 30 seconds?"

Variant B — Specific observation

"Hi Maria, Magnus calling. I was on the HHSC site looking at facilities in [county] and noticed [specific thing]. I built a tool that helps with exactly that — can I take 60 seconds?"

Variant C — Referral adjacency

"Hey Maria, I'm Magnus with ComplianceKit. I've been talking to a bunch of center directors in [state] about inspection prep and wanted to get your perspective. Two minutes?"

Variant D — The question open

"Maria, Magnus here — quick question before I explain why I'm calling: when's your next state inspection?"

Never do this

Never say "How are you today?" or "Did I catch you at a bad time?" These are hang-up triggers. The first flags you as a telemarketer; the second invites them to say yes and escape.

11. Discovery: the 4 questions

If they give you 60 seconds, spend 45 of it listening. Your goal is to learn their pain, not to pitch. A great discovery question produces a 2-minute answer from them. A bad one produces "uh-huh."

The 4 questions, in order

  1. "How do you currently handle inspection prep?"
    Open-ended. Tells you everything about their current workflow, their pain, their vocabulary. Shut up and listen.
  2. "When's your next inspection, and how ready do you feel?"
    Urgency indicator. If it's <60 days and they don't feel ready, you have a fast deal.
  3. "What's the last thing that caught you by surprise during an inspection?"
    This unlocks war stories. Let them vent. Acknowledge with "oh god" and "yeah that's brutal" — don't pitch.
  4. "If you had a magic button that guaranteed you'd pass every inspection without panic, what would it be worth to you a month?"
    Price anchor. They almost always say a number > $99. Perfect.

Listening tactics

  • Silence is your friend. After they answer, wait 2 seconds before speaking. They'll usually add the most valuable thing.
  • Mirror. Repeat back the last 2–3 words of what they said as a question. "...caught you by surprise?" They'll elaborate.
  • Label emotions. "Sounds exhausting." "That must've been stressful." They feel heard, trust builds.
  • Take notes literally. Their phrases become your email copy later.

12. The pitch (90 seconds, plain English)

After discovery, you pitch — but only the parts that match what they told you. This is the single biggest mistake first-time founders make: reciting every feature instead of selling the one they need.

The pitch framework

Problem → Product → Proof → Price. In 90 seconds.

You[Problem — mirror their words] So from what you're telling me, the worst part is [their phrase — "chasing down staff CPR expirations" / "parent files that don't get updated" / "never knowing if you're actually ready"]. You're not alone — every owner I talk to says some version of that.
You[Product — one sentence] ComplianceKit is a dashboard that automatically tracks every compliance requirement the [FL DCF / TX HHSC / CA CCL] cares about, flags anything expiring in the next 90 days, and lets you run a self-inspection that mirrors the actual state checklist so you know exactly where you stand before the inspector arrives.
You[Proof — specific, concrete] Centers using it typically move from a compliance score of around 60 up to 95 in the first 45 days, and the big shift they describe is — they stop losing sleep before inspections.
You[Price — confident, no apologizing] It's $99 a month, no contract, cancel anytime. You can try it for 14 days with no credit card.
You[Ask] Want me to send over a calendar link and walk you through it in 15 minutes?

13. Objection handling — every objection

Objections are not rejection. They are requests for more information. The only bad response is panicking. Memorize these and the fear drops 80%.

Universal objection framework — Acknowledge → Question → Reframe

For every objection: (1) acknowledge their concern is valid (never argue), (2) ask a question that gets them to elaborate, (3) reframe with information that addresses the real concern.

"I'm busy right now."
Response: "Totally get it — running a center is non-stop. I only need 27 seconds to tell you why I called and then you can decide if it's worth any more of your time. If not, I'll get off your phone."
Why it works: You agree with them (no friction), you reassert the micro-commitment, you give them the exit.
"Not interested." (before you've even pitched)
Response: "That's fair — you don't even know what this is yet. Can I ask: is it that you're happy with how you're currently handling inspection prep, or is it more that you're just swamped right now?"
Why it works: Splits the "no" into two diagnostics. The first answer is rare and legitimate. The second reopens the conversation.
"We already have a system."
Response: "Good — most centers I talk to do. Can I ask what you're using? Binder, spreadsheet, another software?" (Listen.) "Got it. Most people who switch to us are coming from [exactly that]. The #1 reason is [state-specific deadline tracking / parent portal / self-inspection]. Worth 15 minutes to compare?"
Why it works: You validate the incumbent, you learn what you're really up against, you anchor on a differentiator they likely don't have.
"It's too expensive."
Response: "Fair — can I ask what you'd expect to pay for something like this? I want to make sure I'm thinking about it the way you are." (Listen.) "Got it. For context — one missed deadline that triggers a DCF/HHSC/CCL violation is typically $500 to $5,000 in fines, not counting what it does to your parent referrals. We're $99 a month. The way most owners frame it is 'this is about six enrollment hours worth of tuition.'"
Why it works: You don't discount. You reframe against the cost of the problem, not the cost of the solution.
"I need to think about it."
Response: "Totally reasonable — it's your money. Just so I'm helpful on the next step: what specifically are you weighing? Is it price, is it whether it'll actually fit how you work, or is it whether you trust us?"
Why it works: "I need to think about it" is almost always a real objection in disguise. Flushing out the real one is the entire game.
"Send me some information."
Response: "Happy to. So I send the right thing — is the main question 'what does this do' or 'what does this cost'? Also, when would be a good time for us to talk for 15 minutes after you've looked at it? Tuesday at 10, or Wednesday at 2?"
Why it works: You comply with their ask but attach a next step. "Send me info" without a follow-up is a graveyard.
"I just passed my inspection."
Response: "Congrats — that's huge. Honestly, the best time to set up a system like this is right after an inspection when you have a year of runway instead of 30 days of panic. Would you want to talk now so you're 10 steps ahead for next year, or would you rather I circle back in 6 months?"
Why it works: Reframes their "no now" as "maybe later" with a specific calendared follow-up. Many of these convert in month 9–10.
"I don't have time to learn a new software."
Response: "That's the #1 objection I hear, and it's legitimate. Here's what I'll do — I'll personally walk you through setup in 20 minutes, and your staff files can be uploaded in under an hour because we auto-extract the data. If after 14 days it's not saving you time, cancel. You've lost nothing."
Why it works: You acknowledge the real fear (switching cost), you personally absorb the risk, you give them a no-lose out.
"I need to talk to my [husband / business partner / accountant]."
Response: "Makes sense. What would be easiest — I set up a short call for the three of us, or I send you a one-page summary you can share with them? Either way I want to make sure you have what you need to make the call."
Why it works: Moves the decision-maker into the conversation or arms the prospect as your internal advocate.
"How do I know you're legit / won't disappear?"
Response: "Fair question — I'd ask the same. Month-to-month, no contract, cancel anytime. Your data is yours and exportable as PDF or CSV whenever you want it, even after canceling. If I go out of business tomorrow, you walk away with your files."
Why it works: Acknowledges the real concern (vendor risk), removes lock-in as a threat.
"My state rep already helps us with compliance."
Response: "Great — reps are really valuable for quarterly check-ins. ComplianceKit is the between-visits piece: it's your always-on scoreboard so you're not finding out something's expired two weeks before your inspection. Think of it as what your rep would want you to have between their visits."
Why it works: Doesn't dismiss the incumbent help, positions as complementary.
"Just email me and I'll look." (said dismissively)
Response: "Will do. Realistically, how often do cold sales emails actually get opened on your side? Mine go in the trash. Want to just do 10 minutes live instead, so you can see it work with your actual state requirements?"
Why it works: Honest, slightly self-deprecating, calls out the brush-off without being rude.
"Who gave you my number?"
Response: "Your facility is on the [DCF / HHSC / CCL] public provider directory — that's where I pulled it from. Happy to take you off my list if you'd rather."
Why it works: Honest and accurate. Some people will say "remove me." That's fine — move on.

14. Closing & next steps

Most first-time founders undersell the close. You don't ask. You hope they ask. They never do. The close is just a specific, time-bound next step — asked confidently.

The only closes you need

The Demo Close (end of cold call)

"Sounds like this is worth a closer look. I've got Tuesday at 10am or Wednesday at 2pm open for a 15-minute walk-through. Which works?"

Why: Two specific options closes better than an open "when's good?" Time-boxing ("15 minutes") lowers the commitment.

The Trial Close (end of demo)

"Based on what I showed you, does this solve the [their exact words] problem you described?"

Why: Gets explicit agreement before asking for the sale. If they say yes, the next question writes itself.

The Start Close (after trial agreement)

"Great. The easiest next step is I send you a signup link, you spin up your account in 5 minutes, and I personally help you upload your first staff file tomorrow morning. Want me to text you the link now?"

Why: Removes all friction, attaches your personal help, frames the first real action as already scheduled.

The Assumptive Close (late-stage)

"Sounds like we're ready to get you started. Do you want to use the same email as the one on your facility's website, or a different one?"

Why: Skips the "yes" and moves straight to logistics. Only use when you have clear buying signals.

Always end with a written next step

Before hanging up, confirm: "I'll send you a calendar invite for Tuesday at 10am and a one-page summary to review beforehand. You'll get both in the next 5 minutes — can you confirm you've got them?" Then actually send them within 5 minutes while the call is fresh.

15. Voicemail strategy

70–85% of your dials will go to voicemail. How you handle voicemail is a multiplier on everything else.

Should you leave a voicemail?

Yes — but only on the second and third attempts, not the first. First dial, hang up at voicemail (no name on caller ID yet). Second dial the next day, leave a short voicemail. Third dial two days later, leave a slightly different voicemail.

The 20-second voicemail

YouHi Maria, Magnus here with ComplianceKit. I'm calling about inspection prep for [facility name] — we built a tool specifically for Florida DCF requirements, and I had something specific I wanted to run by you. I'll send a quick email as well so you have it in writing. You can reach me at 555-123-4567 — again, Magnus at ComplianceKit, 555-123-4567. Talk soon.

Why this voicemail works

  • Uses their name and facility name — proves it's not a robocall.
  • Specific to their state regulator — proves you've done homework.
  • "Had something specific to run by you" — creates curiosity without pitching.
  • Promises follow-up email — lowers the pressure to call back.
  • Phone number twice — at different paces. People miss it the first time.

The voicemail-email combo

Immediately after leaving a voicemail, send a 3-line email with the subject line "Voicemail I just left for [facility name]":

Subject: Voicemail I just left for Sunshine Kids Academy
Maria — just left you a quick voicemail. TL;DR: I built a tool that handles FL DCF inspection prep for centers your size, and I had a specific question about how you're currently tracking staff certification renewals.
15 minutes any day this week? Tuesday 10am or Wednesday 2pm?
— Magnus · ComplianceKit · (phone)

The combination of voicemail + matching email roughly doubles response rate vs. either alone.

16. Gatekeepers & front-desk staff

At a single-site daycare, the "gatekeeper" is usually a lead teacher or assistant director. They're helpful by default but trained not to hand the phone to the owner.

Don't try to fool them. Level with them.

ThemSunshine Kids.

This works because: you're honest (disarms them), you acknowledge it's cold (they appreciate it), you give a time cap (60 seconds), and you give them an exit (bad moment?). About half the time, they'll hand the phone over or take a message.

If they say "she's not available"

If they insist on taking a message

Specificity here ensures the owner has the context when you call back instead of returning a cold call blind.

19. Running the demo call

The demo is not a tour of your UI. It is a guided discovery of whether the product solves their specific problem. If you lecture, you lose.

15-minute demo structure

  1. Minute 0–2: Re-discovery. "Before I show anything, remind me what you're hoping to solve today." Let them re-articulate. You now have the script for the demo.
  2. Minute 2–3: Set the frame. "I'm going to show you three things: how we track [their pain #1], how we handle [their pain #2], and what the self-inspection looks like. Stop me anytime."
  3. Minute 3–12: Show, don't tell. Use their state. Use realistic data. Narrate what you're clicking and, more importantly, what they'd do. "If you were the one logged in right now, you'd see..."
  4. Minute 12–14: Trial close. "Does this solve the [their pain] thing you mentioned?" Listen.
  5. Minute 14–15: Next step. Either start them on a trial, schedule a follow-up with their spouse/partner, or confirm when they'll circle back.

Demo do's and don'ts

Do

  • Use their state, their requirements, their language.
  • Ask "what would you want to do next?" at natural pause points.
  • Acknowledge rough edges honestly — it builds trust.
  • Share screen with ONLY the app visible. Close Slack, email, everything.
  • Send a follow-up email within 30 minutes with a recap + next step.

Don't

  • Open with "let me show you everything we do."
  • Click through features they haven't asked about.
  • Apologize for things they haven't noticed.
  • Over-run 15 minutes without explicit permission.
  • End without a clear, written next step.

20. Onboarding (first 30 days)

The first 30 days determine whether they stay for 7 years or churn in month 3. At $99/mo you can't afford luxurious onboarding, but you can afford precise onboarding.

The first-week playbook

Day 0 (signup)

  • Automated: Welcome email, setup wizard opens immediately.
  • Manual from you: Personal email within 1 hour. Three lines max.
  • Wizard goals: Facility info entered, state selected, one staff file uploaded.

Day 1

  • Calendly link sent: "Want a 15-minute personal walkthrough this week?"
  • If score is below 30, automated nudge: "Here are the 3 fastest ways to bring your score up."

Day 3

  • Check activation state. If no child file + no staff file → manual personal email.
  • If wizard incomplete → nudge with a specific offer to help.

Day 7

  • Send first weekly digest (compliance score, upcoming deadlines, suggested actions).
  • Ask for a signup reason: "What made you try us? (One sentence.)"

Day 14 (trial end / first charge)

  • Pre-charge email 48 hours before: "Here's what you'll be charged. Questions?"
  • Personal note from you day 14: "How's it going? Anything not working?"

Day 30

  • 10-minute check-in call (optional but offered).
  • Ask: "What's working? What's missing? Would you recommend us yet?"

Activation metric (the single number that matters)

Definition: By Day 7, the account has (a) compliance score above 60, and (b) at least one tracked deadline. If both → 85% retained at month 6. If neither → 10% retained at month 6. Everything you do in onboarding is aimed at this metric.

21. Expansion & multi-site upsell

Single-site centers will eventually open a second location, or refer a friend who owns another center. Both are expansion vectors — you just need a listening posture.

Triggers to watch for

  • Customer mentions "we're opening a second location" — congratulate immediately, then offer a 2-month free on the second site if they upgrade at launch.
  • Customer hits 90% compliance score for 90+ days — they're a reference candidate. Ask for a testimonial and a referral.
  • Customer's capacity grows — enrollment rising means they need more staff, which means more files, which means more value from the tool.

The multi-site pitch

Price the Enterprise tier at $199/site/mo with director-level consolidated reporting. When a two-site account signs, upgrade price is lower per-site ($199 vs $99+$99 = $198 — essentially free) but you get a bigger deal locked in. Position it as: "Same price as two Professional accounts, plus you get one dashboard that rolls both sites up."

Referrals

Every activated customer should get a referral ask at month 3 and month 9. Template:

Subject: A small ask
Maria — hope things are good at Sunshine.
I'm at the stage where most growth comes from director-to-director word of mouth. If you know one or two other center owners in [state] who are dealing with the compliance headache, would you be willing to intro me? Even just a name and number.
I'll send anyone you refer a $50 credit on their first month, and knock $50 off your next invoice when they sign up.
— Magnus

22. Churn saves

A customer who is about to cancel is not lost. They're telling you exactly what needs to change. Handled well, 30–50% of cancel requests are save-able.

The churn-save call (within 4 hours of any cancel signal)

  1. Don't fight. Your first sentence is: "Totally — before I process that, can I ask one question so I understand what went wrong?"
  2. Listen for the real reason. "Too expensive" usually means "not enough value." "Didn't use it" means "onboarding failed." "Don't need it" means "we haven't had an inspection scare yet."
  3. Offer a specific remedy, not a discount. Discounts cheapen the product and set a floor. Remedies (free setup call, 30 days free, custom feature) solve the real problem.
  4. If they still want to cancel, make it easy and friendly. "Totally fair. I'll cancel right now. If anything changes in the next 6 months, text me directly — I'll reinstate with any data you had."

Post-cancel follow-up

30 days after cancel, send a single honest email: "Hey Maria — you canceled last month. What's the one thing that would have made you stay? I'm not trying to win you back — I'm trying to not make the same mistake with the next customer." Response rate is often 40%+ and insights are gold.

25. Handling rejection — the real answer

This is the section worth re-reading on a hard day.

You are going to have days where 30 people in a row blow you off. You'll have afternoons where someone is openly rude and it sits in your chest for six hours. Every founder who has done this has those days. It is the job.

The things that actually help

  1. Separate the person from the outcome. They are not rejecting you. They don't know you. They're rejecting an interruption to their day. You would too.
  2. Log the "no" as data, not a wound. Put it in your sheet. "No — said they use a spreadsheet and are fine." Now you have intel. The wound converts to a asset.
  3. Celebrate dials, not deals, during the first 30 days. You control dials. You don't control deals. Reward the controllable metric while the other one builds.
  4. Find one person who can hear the hard calls. Another founder, a spouse, a friend. Text them after a brutal stretch: "Just got yelled at by a daycare owner in Tampa, sending strength." Get a 🤝 back. Keep going.
  5. Stop on a win. End every calling block on a connection, even a short one. If the last call was a painful one, dial one more and have any human conversation before closing the laptop.
  6. Remember why you built it. There is a daycare owner tonight who is going to stay up until 1am looking for a CPR certification in a filing cabinet, and she is going to fail her inspection tomorrow and pay a $2,000 fine she doesn't have. You built a thing that would solve that for her. Every call is a chance to find her. That is not an annoying sales job. That is useful work.
The truth

Most founders don't fail because their product is bad. They fail because they stop selling. You don't need to be a natural. You don't need a sales background. You need to dial the phone, say your opening, listen, and do it again tomorrow. That's the whole game. You can do this. The only question is whether you'll let yourself.
